1. What Is PACT?

“A class is a story someone else already wrote. Power is a blank page.” — Maela Trune, wandering loremaster

PACT in 60 seconds
You earn Advancement Points (AP) by adventuring, then spend them on a price list, hit points, a skill, a feat, a spell, a class feature, one purchase at a time. Almost everything in the game is on that list — a class feature, a spell, a proficiency, even another class’s signature trick — so very little is truly off-limits: if it exists in the core rules, you can nearly always find a price for it.
Nothing is free and no one can afford everything, so every hero comes out different.
That is the whole game. Everything else in this book is just the price list and how to read it.

PACT lets you build and grow a Dungeons & Dragons character without classes and levels deciding everything for you. You don't level up and collect a fixed package of features. Instead, you earn a pool of Advancement Points (AP) and spend them on exactly the abilities you want — more hit points, a sharper sword, a new spell, a class feature you've always envied.

PACT sits on top of the 2024 edition of D&D. It uses the twelve core classes as menus of features. Everything else works just as it does in the core rulebooks — the dice, the spells, the monsters, the maths. PACT changes only how you gain your abilities. You buy them one at a time, from clear price lists.

What changes from the 2024 PHB. PACT keeps the core rules but deliberately departs in a handful of places: your level is the number of Hit Dice you own; every Hit Die is a d6; nothing is free; classes are feature menus you unlock, not levels you climb; and several new subsystems (Boons, Drawbacks, and Bindings) carry their own rules. Every departure is listed — with its 2024 baseline and the reason for the change — in the House Rules & Departures appendix at the back. DMs should read it before play.

The two golden rules
1. Nothing is free. Hit points, saving throws, skills, class features, and magic are all purchases. Because every purchase competes for the same points, no character can own everything, and every character ends up genuinely unique.
2. Prices are read 'before the increase.' Many costs rise with how many of a thing you already own. You always pay the price for the count you have before this purchase is added. Buying your 3rd skill? You pay the price for 'already own 2,' then you own 3.

Why build with points?

Standard play hands every character a class and a level. With them comes a fixed list of features on a fixed schedule. That is fast and familiar. But it also means two fighters come out nearly identical. A concept that spans two classes is awkward to build. And what you are handed is rarely what you actually wanted. PACT replaces the schedule with a budget. You decide what your hero is good at, then pay for those things and nothing else.

Three benefits follow. Flexibility: any feature from any of the twelve classes is on the menu, so a holy duellist or a spell-slinging scout is just a shopping list, not a multiclass puzzle. Control: your character grows in the direction you steer it, one purchase at a time, rather than waiting for a level to hand you something. Balance: every advantage has a price drawn from one master table, so power is measured in points. A DM can set a budget and trust that two characters built to it are a fair match, whatever each one chose to buy.

Points bring a few quieter advantages too. Because a character grows one purchase at a time, players feel the weight of each gain. They stay attached to a hero they assembled themselves, rather than one handed a fat package of features at every level-up. A PACT character also need not begin at full 1st-level power. You can open below that — a green recruit who is genuinely fragile — and let the heroes earn their competence. That turns the dangerous low levels into a place a party can linger for many sessions without the game feeling stuck, because every session still brings a purchase to look forward to. It also gives gold and downtime real work to do. With the optional economy (sections 17 and 18), they gate and shape what you can buy, so treasure and a quiet week of training finally matter.

The high end rewards the climb too. From around level 15, the rarest top-tier boons and capstones come within reach. A character can chase these, or use some of those AP to round themselves out — shoring up a weak save, broadening their skills, or leaning harder into the role the campaign has shaped for them.

Who this guide is for

This is a complete beginner's guide. If you have played any D&D before, you already know 90% of what you need — PACT just hands you the shopping cart. If you are brand new, read sections 1–5 carefully. Then follow the worked examples in section 3 and copy their shape.

PACT is at its best in a homebrew campaign. There the DM sets the budget, the treasure, and the pace, and can tune the system to the table. It also rewards a little experience behind the screen. You should be comfortable judging what an unusual request is worth, and saying yes or no to it. If you mean to run it inside a published adventure, read the DM's section (section 17) first. Those modules assume standard levels and downtime, so set the budget and pacing deliberately before play begins.

2. The Three Currencies

“Talent is bought with three coins: power, gold, and the long patience of practice.” — the Ledger of Aptitudes

Every advancement in PACT costs three resources at once. You will think about the first one most. The other two keep advancement grounded in the fiction.

Advancement Points (AP)

AP are the heart of the system. Your DM awards them for adventuring, roughly on the schedule in the advancement-pace table (section 17). As a rough guide, about 491 AP builds a full level-20 character — section 17 has the full advancement-pace curve. In the stories throughout this book, AP often go by a plainer name: power. It is what a hero earns, and spends, to become more. PACT generally favours spellcasters: because you buy your magic piece by piece, a caster can assemble exactly the spells, slots, and tricks they want, and that flexibility can be disproportionately powerful. The higher price is the counterweight — pure casters average out dearer than martials at the upper levels, by deliberate design, so the flexibility the system hands them stays in balance.

Gold (gp)

Training, materials, and rare components cost money. Most purchases have a gold price, read off the universal training band in section 16. Section 17 covers how DMs pace gold rewards through a campaign and when to switch the economy on or off.

Downtime (Time)

Mastery takes weeks of practice or study. Most purchases also cost downtime — training time, study, and practice — from the band in section 16. Section 17 explains how downtime fits the rhythm of a campaign and why the time costs scale as they do. This is the 'T' in PACT. Time is a real cost, not an afterthought.

❧ Three Coins
“There are only three coins in the world,” Hatch told the apprentice, laying them on the bar one by one. “Power, which is what you are. Gold, which is what you have. And time, which is what you spend whether you mean to or not.”
The boy reached for the gold. Hatch covered it with his hand. “Everyone grabs that one first, and everyone is wrong. Gold you can steal back. Power you earn slow and keep. But time, lad, time is the only coin the gods will not let you counterfeit, and the only one you cannot get more of. Spend the first two like water if you must. Spend the third like blood.”
— Hatch, retired fence, on the three currencies
Character generation is free of gold and time. Anything you buy when you first build your character costs only AP, it is assumed you already trained for it in your backstory. Gold and downtime only start mattering for things you buy during play.

3. Character Creation, Step by Step

“First you decide who you are. Then you spend until the ledger and the dream agree.” — Hatch, retired fence

Here is the whole process. Each step links to a later section for prices and detail.

Pick a concept. Decide who your character is in plain words: 'a tough greatsword soldier,' 'a sneaky knife-fighter,' 'a battlefield healer.'

Start with stats of 10. Every ability score begins at 10. You don't roll, and you don't point-buy. (section 5.)

Optionally take drawbacks and dumps for AP. Lower any stat by 2 to gain +4 AP, down to a floor of 6. You can also take drawbacks from section 14. (sections 5 & 14.)

Pick one origin class now, at character creation. This is your 'home' class. You always have one, and you choose it before you spend a single point. Its features are bought free of the cross-class surcharge that every other class's features carry. (section 11.)

Spend your AP. This is the heart of creation. In any mix you like, your points buy: Hit Dice (which set your level and hit points); ability increases; saving throws; skills and languages; feats; weapon and armour proficiency; class features; and, if you want magic, a spellcasting stack of Foundation, Rank, cantrips, slots and spells. (sections 4 to 13.) Nothing is free. You begin with no skills, no racial traits, no class features and no starting kit. The Player’s Handbook starting equipment and background perks do not apply. A 2024 character origin is redundant here, since the skills, tool proficiency, origin feat, and ability-score increases it bundles are all things PACT makes you buy. So pick a background for story and colour if you like, but it grants none of them for free. Every advantage on your sheet must be bought with AP.

Optionally convert a little AP into starting gold for gear and coin. (section 16.)

Record your character and play. As you earn more AP, keep shopping — but now gold and downtime apply.

Locked at character creation
A few choices can only be made when you first build your character, never added later in play:
☐ Dumping stats for AP (lowering a score below 10 for points).
☐ Taking drawbacks for AP (section 14).
☐ Your origin class, and the optional second origin class (14 AP).
☐ Your origin race, and the optional second origin race.
☐ Traits from a race that is not your origin (the +1 cross-race buys).
Everything else, skills, feats, Hit Dice, class features, spells, magic, can be bought at creation or earned later in play.
Your starting budget, set by your DM
Your DM sets the budget, and PACT now gives one for every level. The standard track starts a fresh Level 1 hero at 79 AP, with 24 AP a level, reaching 535 AP at Level 20. A more generous track adds 28 AP a level (Level 1 = 83, Level 20 = 615) for tables that want characters nearer a complete build for their level. Level 0 — an optional apprentice or prelude tier — starts at 55 AP. A Level-0 character still buys one Hit Die and is fully playable; they have simply not yet grown into a complete Level-1 hero. This schedule is built on four sessions per level at six to seven AP a session; increase or decrease that cadence to match how fast your table levels, and pick one track for the whole campaign. The level-1 worked examples in section 3 walk through the build process step by step.
Why start at 79? A by-the-book level-1 hero — every proficiency, a full kit, maxed stats — prices out around 90 AP. But much of that is abilities a given character never uses: heavy armour on a duellist, the whole martial arsenal on someone who only ever swings one blade. A reasonable level-1 character, built with sensible dumps and a fitting drawback or two, comes in well under that — most classes land between 35 and 80 AP for their playable core. The standard 79-AP budget covers any class with room to round it out; the generous 83 leaves a little more. Either way you arrive close to a complete hero for your level and grow steadily from there. For a written campaign, consider a short Level 0 prelude — a session or two for the characters to meet and find their feet — before the main story begins. By the time the plot bites, they will have grown into the level-1 heroes the module expects.

Worked Example: spending a session’s AP
You finish Session 4 and the DM awards 7 AP. Your Level-4 fighter already has STR 16, all martial weapons, one Weapon Mastery, and CON 14. Four legal ways to spend the windfall:
☐ Bank toward your 5th Hit Die. The die costs 5 AP and takes you to Level 5; carry the spare 2 AP forward.
☐ Broaden out: a second Weapon Mastery (3 AP) + a third skill (1 AP), then convert the last 3 AP to 150 gp.
☐ Toughen up: raise Constitution 14→16 (6 AP). Your CON modifier rises to +3, adding +1 HP to every Hit Die you own. Bank the spare 1 AP.
☐ Learn a trick: buy Savage Attacker (Arts & Techniques, T1 At-Will, 4 AP) and bank 3 AP.
Notice what you cannot do yet: Extra Attack is a T4 feature (14 AP) gated behind 5 Hit Dice, so it is out of reach until you buy that fifth die. The lesson — not every reward is a level. AP is a slow drip you spend where your hero is heading.

4. Tiers, Levels, and Hit Dice

“Count a warrior's dice and you know their measure. Every die is a year survived — a lesson kept.” — the Ledger of Aptitudes

Character Level = Hit Dice

In PACT, your character level is just the number of Hit Dice you own. Buy your fifth Hit Die and you are level 5. Any feature that requires 'level 5' now unlocks, and you may buy your proficiency bonus up to +3 (section 6). There is one exception: the level of spells you can cast comes from your Spellcasting Rank (section 13), not your Hit Dice. Any core rule that mentions level means this one. A Level-0 apprentice is the one step below this: it owns a single Hit Die and is fully playable, but is not yet a complete Level-1 hero.

❧ Counting Battles
In the war-lodges of the north they do not ask a fighter their age. They ask how many battles they walked away from. “A man's years are luck,” the old chief said, “but his battles are earned, each one a fight he should not have walked away from and a lesson he was too stubborn to forget. Count a warrior's battles and you know their measure better than any title. Six is a hard road walked. Twenty is a legend, or a liar.”
He had fought through forty. He never said a word about it; he did not need to. The young ones counted, and were quiet.
— from the saga-counts of the Bear-Lodge

Advancing HD

Changing your choices on level-up. Each time you gain a Hit Die, you may swap up to two of the choices the 2024 rules let you change on a level-up — a known spell, a cantrip, a fighting style, a weapon mastery, or a class or subclass pick. If the new choice costs the same or less than the old one, the swap is free; if it costs more, you pay only the difference, and the AP you already spent carries across to the new pick. Outside a level-up you can still change a learned choice, but it costs full price. You pay the new pick’s whole AP cost, and the AP you spent on the old choice is gone. Swapping a known 1st-level spell (1 AP) for a 4th-level one, for example, costs the full 2 AP, and that first point is lost. (A subclass’s always-prepared spells are set by the subclass and can’t be swapped either way.)

Tiers (T1–T7)

Every purchasable feature has a Tier from T1 to T7. The tier sets both its price and the character level you must reach to buy it. A feature's tier roughly matches the D&D level at which you'd normally gain it. T1 is a level-1 feature; T7 is a true level-17+ capstone.

The Hit-Dice gate (read this twice)
You can never buy an ability before you own the Hit Dice, and hence the equivalent level, it requires. A Tier sets a minimum number of Hit Dice (the table above), and you must already own that many dice to make the purchase, no saving up a T4 feature at level 4.
The same gate applies to magic. A spell of a given level needs a caster of the matching level, exactly as in core D&D, so a 2nd-level spell needs 3 Hit Dice, a 3rd-level spell needs 5, and so on. The full spell-level → Hit-Dice table is in section 13.
The gate is a floor, not a guarantee. Clearing the Hit-Dice (and Rank) requirement makes a purchase legal, not automatically available, your DM may story-gate any ability, especially top-tier powers and the highest-level spells, behind an in-fiction quest before you may buy it at all (section 16).
Not everything has a Tier. The universal purchases, Hit Dice, saving throws, skills, feats, ability score increases, weapon mastery, and armour and weapon proficiency, carry no Tier, so this gate never touches them. Heavy armour, for instance, is dearer than light, but it's available from level 1 (Appendix C).

Hit points

Your first Hit Die gives 6 HP; each die after that gives 3 more — a lean, class-neutral chassis you raise with Vigor or Constitution. These totals are fixed, never rolled; you roll a Hit Die only when you spend one to heal on a rest. Add your Constitution modifier for every die you own. That same bonus — Constitution plus every rank of Vigor — also applies each time you spend a die to heal, not just when you first gain it (see the callout below).

Vigor and Constitution heal you on every rest
When you spend a Hit Die to recover hit points on a short or long rest, you roll the die (a d6) and add your Constitution modifier and your full Vigor bonus to the HP regained. The die heals exactly as if it were granting fresh hit points. A tough hero therefore mends as quickly as they soak: every rank of Vigor you bought makes each rest-time Hit Die heal +1 HP on top of the roll, for every die you spend.
(The lone exception is the Slow to Mend drawback in section 14, which strips precisely this bonus, its bearer recovers only the raw die.)
Two HP rules worth boxing
One die for everyone. PACT uses a single d6 Hit Die for every class — there are no class-based hit dice. A 5th-level wizard and a 5th-level barbarian with the same Constitution have the same base HP. Durability is bought with Vigor and Grit, not handed out by class.
Total HP = 6 + 3 × (HD − 1) + (CON mod × HD) + (Vigor ranks × HD) + (Grit purchases × 4). The first Hit Die gives 6; each later die gives 3; Constitution adds its modifier to every die; each Vigor rank adds 1 per die; each Grit purchase adds a flat 5.

Ability scores start at 10

Every score begins at 10, no array, no rolling. From there you have two levers.

Dumping for points (character creation only)

At creation, lower any stat by 2 to gain +4 AP immediately. You may do this to as many stats as you like, down to a soft floor of 6. A kept weakness is free fuel for a real strength.

Dumping limits, DM approval and the +20 cap
A stat of 6 is the normal floor, you can dump to 6 freely. Dropping a score below 6 is possible but requires your DM's approval, because a 5-or-lower ability is a serious, roleplay-shaping handicap. Treat it as a story choice you clear with the table, not an automatic AP tap.
There is no hard limit on how many stats you dump, but the total AP you gain from dumping should not exceed +20 without your DM's agreement. Past that you are no longer building a character so much as farming points, and the DM should step in. As with going below 6, more is possible only with the DM's say-so.

Raising stats: the Ability Increase ladder

Buying +2 to a stat (an Ability Increase) gets steeper the higher you climb. From 10 upward the steps cost 4, 5, 7, 10, and 14 AP — 4 for 10→12, then 5, 7, 10, and 14 on up to 20 (40 AP in all). Below 10, buying a dumped stat back up costs 4 AP plus the absolute value of the modifier before the step, so a deep dump is dear to undo. A single +1 costs half the matching +2 step, rounded up.

6. Saving Throws & Proficiency

“Anyone can throw a punch. The rare few learn never to be hit by the world itself.” — the Whispering School

Saving Throws

Sometimes a danger gives you a chance to resist — a gout of fire, a wave of fear, a creeping poison. When it does, you make a saving throw. Roll a d20, add the matching ability modifier, and add your proficiency bonus too if you are proficient in that save. Meet or beat the effect's save DC and you avoid the worst of it. Fall short and you suffer it in full.

There are six saves, one for each ability: Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma. Nothing grants a proficient save for free. You still roll a save you are not proficient in — you simply add no proficiency bonus. That bonus can be the difference between shrugging off a dragon's breath and taking it square in the chest.

7. Skills, Tools & Languages

“A second talent is half the trouble of the first. A fourth is barely any trouble at all.” — Hatch, retired fence

Skills

Skills are cheap and gentle — they climb on the Gentle curve — so even a points-pinching brute can be good at a few things. You start with no free skills; every skill is a purchase. Your next skill costs half the number of skills you already own, rounded down — the 'before-increase' rule. That makes your first two skills free of AP, and prices creep up slowly after that:

Cumulative: 1 skill = 0 AP, 2 = 0, 3 = 1, 4 = 2, 5 = 4, 6 = 6 AP. Expertise — doubling your proficiency on one skill — is bought on a Steady curve, a rising ladder. Your Nth skill Expertise costs N + 2 AP — 3, 4, 5, 6, 7… — on top of owning the skill. So a pair of Expertises is cheap (3 + 4 = 7). But a true skill-master pays steadily more for each one, and there is no cap beyond what you can afford. (The class features that simply hand out Expertise — the rogue's and bard's — grant nothing free here; like any skill, you buy it on this ladder.)

Why the first skills cost 0 AP
'No free skills' means none are handed to you by your class, species, or background, you must still choose to buy each one, and each one occupies a line on your sheet. The first two simply happen to sit at a 0-AP price point. Spend a few AP and a generalist can rack up six.

Tools and Instruments

Tools and instruments, a separate ladder. Tool and musical-instrument proficiencies form a separate pool from skills, and none of them are free. Each costs ⌊owned in the pool ÷ 2⌋ + 1 AP — half the number you already own, rounded down, plus one — so 1 / 1 / 2 / 2 / 3 …, climbing on its own tally. Skills keep their two free picks; tools and instruments do not. You can also gain Expertise with a tool you are proficient in, doubling your proficiency bonus with it. It uses the same Steady ladder as skill Expertise, but on its own separate tally: your Nth tool Expertise costs N + 2 AP (3 / 4 / 5 …), kept quite apart from any skill Expertise you hold.

8. Armour, Weapons & Attunement

“A trick is worth more than a muscle, and a weapon that knows one trick is worth two that don’t.” — Hatch, retired fence

Armour proficiency

Armour is a proficiency like any other, bought on its own short ladder. You pay once for the right to wear a whole category without penalty. After that, any armour in that category is yours to wear. Like weapons, armour takes no class discount — the prices below are flat for everyone.

Custom rule: Strength 10 for medium armour. Medium armour — and the heavy armour that stacks on top of it — needs a Strength score of at least 10 for its protection to apply. A character who has dumped Strength below 10 gains no benefit from medium or heavy armour until they raise it, however many points they have spent on the proficiency. Light armour and shields have no Strength requirement.

Why this rule exists. The 2024 armour table asks nothing of Strength for medium armour — every medium set, up to Half Plate, lists a blank where heavy armour lists Strength 13 to 15. In a points economy, that leaves a door open. A hero can drop Strength to 8, pay the same flat proficiency price as anyone, and strap on Half Plate for AC 15 + 2 Dexterity — heavy-grade protection bought with Dexterity and a dump stat alone, paying nothing into the muscle the plate is meant to demand. Requiring Strength 10 — an unremarkable, average score that costs no honest build a thing — shuts that door. It bites only the deliberate dump, and keeps the medium-and-heavy line a Strength investment, not a free ride on Dexterity. Nothing is free applies to defence too.

The categories stack. You must own each lighter tier before buying the next: Medium requires Light, and Heavy requires Medium. A shield stands alone — buy it whenever you like. Full plate is therefore the whole climb: Light (2) + Medium (4) + Heavy (6) = 12 AP, just as every martial weapon builds on Simple.

Class never gates armour in PACT. A wizard may strap on plate, and a barbarian may go bare. What stops most casters is arithmetic, not law: 12 AP for heavy proficiency, a Strength they likely dumped, and the many spells that need a free hand or somatic gestures. Before you spend a point on plate, remember the cheapest defence of all — Mage Armor and a good Dexterity ask nothing of this ladder.

Weapon proficiency

Before a weapon’s mastery comes its plain proficiency — whether you add your proficiency bonus to attacks with it. Like armour, it is a flat, class-neutral purchase: the same price for everyone, in PACT it is your features that define you, not the equipment you pick up. It carries no Tier and no Hit-Dice gate. You can always attack with any weapon untrained; you simply don’t add your proficiency bonus until you’ve paid for it.

Simple weapons: 1 AP — clubs, daggers, spears, maces, and the like.

One martial weapon: 2 AP (after Simple) — proficiency in a single martial weapon of your choice, such as a longsword or a rapier.

All martial weapons: 8 AP (after Simple) — the whole martial arsenal in one purchase.

Improvised weapons: 3 AP — proficiency to wield improvised weapons such as a chair leg, a bottle, or a thrown rock, adding your proficiency bonus to the attack (the trick the Tavern Brawler feat grants). It is a flat, stand-alone purchase that needs no category first.

Buy single weapons if you favour one or two. The full bundle only pays off once you want four or more (three singles cost 6, the bundle 8). For example, a rogue who just wants a rapier pays Simple 1 + one martial 2 = 3 AP. Full prices and a quick lookup are in Appendix C.

Weapon Mastery

The 2024 weapon-mastery properties are unbundled from class and sold one at a time. The first mastery costs 2 AP, and each one after rises by one: 2 / 3 / 4 / 5 / 6…

The eight properties — Cleave, Graze, Nick, Push, Sap, Slow, Topple, Vex — are each tied to specific weapons in the core rules.

Mastery needs proficiency, not a martial weapon
A weapon’s mastery is the advanced trick, and you can only buy it for a weapon you are already proficient with — but that proficiency can be simple. Many simple weapons carry a mastery of their own: a Dagger’s Nick, a Mace’s Sap, a Quarterstaff’s Topple, a Handaxe’s Vex. So a character who owns only simple-weapon proficiency can still buy a mastery — no martial weapon required. A martial mastery simply stacks on martial proficiency: a wizard who covets a greatsword’s Graze pays the martial-weapon proficiency and the mastery, while a fighter who already wields greatswords just adds the mastery. Proficiency first, then the trick.

Armour proficiency is bought the same flat, class-neutral way. Each category’s cost (Shield, Light, Medium, Heavy) is listed in Appendix C alongside the weapon table.

Attunement Slots

Magic items that demand attunement are potent. Stacking several is one of the surest ways a character's power balloons, so the right to attune is something you buy. There is no free slot and no upper limit. Each attunement slot is bought on the Steep ladder — the same +2-per-step climb as feats. Your first slot costs 4 AP, and every slot after costs 2 more.

9. The Master Cost Table

“Power is never dear because of what it does, only because of how often it lets you do it.” — Archmagister Vel

Every class feature is priced by two things. The first is its Tier — how strong it is, and how late it arrives. The bracketed character level beside each tier shows the level at which a feature of that tier is normally reached. The second is its availability band — how often you actually get to use it. Find the tier down the side and the band across the top. Where they meet is the sticker price, before any origin discount or cross-class surcharge. The rule of thumb is simple: at a given Tier, the more often an ability is available, the more it costs. The last column, Premium, is the dearest of all. The Master Cost Table prices every named class and subclass feature by its Tier and Band. The three cost curves: Gentle, Steady, Steep

The five availability bands

These bands are a guide, not a rigid formula. Pricing how often a feature is available is sometimes a judgement call — two designers might reasonably place the same ability in different bands. Where PACT has placed a feature might not match your own instinct; treat it as a starting point and adjust for your table.

Every feature falls into one of five bands. Four of them answer a single question — how often do you actually get to use this? They run from cheapest (least available) to dearest (always on). The fifth, Premium, sits above them all. It is the dearest band, reserved for the strongest features. Unlike the four uptime bands you don't route a feature there with a question; the designers place it deliberately (the box “Why Premium sits above Passive” explains why, and the feature tables mark which ones). 10. Species and Heritage Packs

“Blood is a gift you did not pay for. Everything after, you earn.” — the Ledger of Aptitudes

Left to the open market, a starting hero could barely afford to be their species once they had bought stats and a weapon. The traits that define an ancestry simply cost too much against a fresh budget. PACT fixes this with Heritage Packs — a small, subsidised bundle of the traits that truly define a species, sold cheap so every character can look like their ancestry from day one.

To be a species, you must buy its pack
A Heritage Pack is not optional flavour, it is what makes you that species. To play a dwarf you buy the Dwarf pack; to play an elf you buy the Elf pack. Without the pack you simply are not that race, and you gain none of its defining traits. The single exception is the Human, whose pack is empty and free: humans are the blank slate, so 'buying the Human pack' costs nothing and instead grants +2 AP to spend freely.

How Heritage Packs work

Each species has one Heritage Pack, and it holds only the race-defining basics — the things that make a dwarf a dwarf (darkvision, poison-hardy) or a halfling a halfling (small, nimble).

A pack's price is a flat, subsidised cost, not the sum of its parts. Every species pack costs exactly 5 AP. That is still well below the price of buying the same traits one by one, so low-level characters can afford their birthright and still build a hero.

Everything beyond the basics is bought normally. A human's bonus skill, a tiefling's innate spells, an elf's extra cantrip — none of these are in the pack. You buy them on the ordinary skill, feature, or spell ladders.

Packs are balanced against one another, so no species is a must-pick. You take a pack once, at character creation, free of gold and downtime.

Why packs are subsidised
The pack price is deliberately lower than the Master-Table value of its traits, a dwarf's basics are worth around 9 AP à la carte but cost 5 in the pack. PACT eats that difference so a brand-new hero can spend most of their 50-AP budget on who they choose to be, class features, stats, magic, instead of paying full freight just to have their birthright. Humans get no pack, but they also pay nothing and lose nothing: their blank-slate flexibility is the trade.

Traits outside the pack

Your origin race. Just as you have an origin class, your Heritage Pack sets your origin race. Its pack and traits are bought at their normal prices. Traits from any other race cost +1 AP each (every racial trait is T1, so the cross-race surcharge is just +1). Like dumps and drawbacks, other-race traits may be taken only at character creation. Once play begins, you can no longer add a different race's abilities. This keeps a hero rooted in one heritage instead of cherry-picking the best trait from every species.

Worked example, buying across races
Tobias is a Human. He admires a dwarf companion’s Stonecunning and wants it for himself. Stonecunning is a T1 racial trait, so it normally costs its Master-Table price; because Dwarf is not Tobias’s origin race, he pays the cross-race surcharge of +1 on top (every racial trait is T1, so the surcharge is always just +1). He may do this only at character creation. Note the split. A racial trait that simply grants a skill — an elf’s Keen Senses, say — is bought on the skill ladder instead, with no surcharge, because skills are universal. It is the race-defining abilities (darkvision, Stonecunning, a breath weapon) that carry the +1 when taken from a race that is not your own.

Buying a racial ability in play. Race-defining traits are T1 only when taken at character creation. Buy one later, during play, and it is priced at the Tier matching your current Hit Dice. The longer you wait, the dearer it gets. (Non-origin-race abilities can't be added in play at all; only your origin race stays open.) Example: an orc skips Savage Critical at creation, then wants it at 8 Hit Dice. As a creation buy it is a T1 Passive (5 AP). Bought in play at Tier 4, it costs the T4 Passive price of 14 AP instead. Exception — traits gated to a higher level. Some traits cannot be taken at creation; they unlock only at a set level (the larger breath dice at levels 5/11/17, Goliath’s Large Form, higher-rank spells, and the like). Buying one the moment it unlocks is not a “late” buy, so it carries no late-buy surcharge — you pay its flat price (the breath-die steps, for instance, are a flat 4 AP each). The surcharge above applies only to a creation-available trait you chose to defer.

Why it costs more isn't about age. Your 8-Hit-Dice hero may be only months older than the day they set out. Race-defining gifts awaken in childhood, woven into how a body grows and a mind forms. Reaching for one you never developed means forcing an instinct your blood never learned to express. That uphill struggle is exactly what the higher Tier represents. It is always hard to grow into your heritage late.

A second origin race (character creation only). For half-blood and dual-heritage concepts, you may declare a second origin race at creation for double its Heritage Pack price — 10 AP for a Dwarf or a Halfling, and so on. You may then buy that race's Heritage Pack and any of its traits at base price, with no cross-race +1 surcharge, exactly as for your first race. Like every racial choice, it is locked at character creation and cannot be added later. One restriction: Human may not be taken as a second origin race. Its pack is empty and free, so allowing it as a second race would hand you a no-cost licence to cherry-pick another ancestry’s traits at base price — exactly the abuse this rule prevents. If you want to be Human, it must be your first origin race.

Character size (free, set by race). Your size is fixed by your origin race and never costs AP. Most races are Medium. A character whose origin pack is Halfling or Gnome is Small. A Halfling is always Small, even when a second origin race is taken — the small frame is the whole point of the heritage. The Gnome is the one exception: a Gnome who also takes a second origin race (any other, Medium, race) may choose to be either Small or Medium, since gnomish builds run both ways. Whichever you pick, every size — Small or Medium — costs 0 AP.

Anything a species grants beyond its defining basics is paid for like everything else. Each extra trait is priced off the Master Cost Table by its Tier and Band, then bought once, flat, with no class discount or surcharge.

Racial innate spells — a tiefling's Hellish Rebuke, a drow's Faerie Fire — are not free, but they are cheap for your origin race. Buy them as innate magic (§ 12) at half the standard innate price: a 1/long-rest 1st-level spell costs 6 AP, a 2nd-level 9 AP, a 3rd-level 12 AP. Racial cantrips cost the full 4 AP but never count against your cantrip cap. Spell-likes from any other race pay the full innate price plus the cross-race surcharge.

If a species has multiple lineages — Elf (Drow, High, Wood), Gnome (Forest, Rock), Tiefling (Infernal, Abyssal, Chthonic), Dragonborn (one ancestry) — you choose one at character creation and may only buy that lineage's abilities. The full list with prices is in Appendix B.

Any species trait that would grant a free skill or expertise instead sends you to the skill ladder (§ 7) — buy the skill there at its normal price.

11. Classes: Origin, Unlock, Discount

“Your first calling is in your blood; every calling after, you buy with sweat at the door.” — Maela Trune, wandering loremaster

PACT treats the twelve core classes purely as menus of features. You never 'level up in a class'. Instead, you unlock a class's menu, then buy its features one at a time.

Your origin class

Every character has one origin class, chosen at character creation — there is no class-free state. Its features get a permanent discount (the origin adjustment below). Just as important, they are the only class features you may buy without the cross-class surcharge that every other class's features carry.

Why you choose your origin at creation
It's true the origin discount is −(Tier − 1), floor 1, so at T1 it's −0, your very first features aren't any cheaper for being your origin's. But the mirror still bites: a feature from any other class carries the cross-class surcharge (+Tier, floor 1), which is +1 even at T1. So your origin choice matters from your very first purchase, it decides whose features you buy at sticker price and whose cost the surcharge. There is no neutral 'unaligned' option that dodges both, which is why you commit at creation rather than drifting in surcharge-land.

Subclasses

Just as you unlock classes, you choose subclasses within them — a Cleric’s Divine Domain, a Warlock’s Patron, a Fighter’s Martial Archetype. PACT treats a subclass as a small menu, not a single package. Choosing a subclass grants you nothing by itself — it only opens the menu. Every ability on it must be bought individually with AP at its own price: its expanded spell list, each named feature, and any cantrip it grants. A subclass you have opened but spent no points in does nothing for you.

Your first subclass in each class you can build from is free to open — but opening it is all that’s free. Pick it, then buy each piece you want — its expanded spell list (a purchase in its own right; see §13) and any of its named abilities — at their normal prices. The origin discount applies if the subclass belongs to your origin class; the cross-class surcharge applies if it does not. Every other subclass in that class is locked.

To open a second subclass in the same class, you pay a one-time Subclass Unlock of 15 AP — however many you already have. Opening all four subclasses of a class therefore runs 45 AP. The unlock is the only extra cost. The abilities inside are bought at their ordinary prices, and your origin discount still applies.

This is what stops cherry-picking. Reaching into a second subclass for a single signature ability costs 15 AP before you even buy the ability — a poor trade for one toy, but a fair tax once you invest in three or four of that subclass’s features. Breadth is available; idle dabbling is not. The per-class menus in Appendix A list each subclass’s abilities with their prices, so you can pick à la carte.

Origin discount vs. cross-class surcharge

Why the discount? PACT has no locked classes — any character may buy any feature from any class’s menu. But without some cost signal, a hero could cherry-pick the single strongest ability from twelve different disciplines and develop none of them deeply. The origin discount is the system’s answer: it rewards commitment. A character who has trained as a fighter their whole life picks up new fighting techniques a little faster and cheaper than someone who only dabbles, because they already speak that language. The discount is not a gate — cross-class features are always available — it simply means that depth costs less than breadth.

This is the main place where ‘class’ changes a price. It applies to class features and, uniquely, to the spell economy of your origin class’s Discipline (section 13). There the origin discount lowers Foundation, Rank, cantrips, slots, and spells by a flat 1 AP (floor 1). It never touches the truly universal ladders — Hit Dice, saving throws, skills, feats, or ability-score increases — nor any Discipline that is not your origin’s. Origin features are cheaper: subtract (Tier − 1) AP — the Tier minus one, so a T4 origin feature is −3 — to a floor of 1. A T1 origin feature is full price, but a T4 origin feature is −3 AP.

Cross-class features cost more: add the Tier as a surcharge (T1 +1 … T7 +7) — the exact mirror of the discount. The 1-AP floor
No purchase in PACT ever costs less than 1 AP, no matter how many discounts stack on it. Origin discount, Magically Bound, a mentor's price cut, pile them all on and the cost still bottoms out at 1 AP. Anything that's already cheap simply stays at 1; the discounts just stop biting once you hit the floor. The floor only bites purchases whose sticker price is already 1 AP or more. The few things that are free by design, your first two skills and your first new language, have a sticker price of 0 and stay free; the floor never bumps a 0-AP item up to 1.

Features that improve: stepped vs. premium

Many class features get stronger at higher levels, but they do not all grow the same way, so PACT splits them into two kinds. Knowing which kind you own tells you whether you pay again.

Stepped features come in named, discrete versions that you actively choose. Examples: Sneak Attack 1d6 → 2d6 → 3d6; a first Extra Attack, then a second; a Martial Arts die growing d6 → d8 → d10 → d12; a Monk's Unarmored Movement climbing +10 → +15 → +20 ft. Each named step is a separate purchase at the tier it unlocks. You do not get the bigger version for free. Extra Attack does not stack across classes. The basic Extra Attack grants a single second attack, so if you already own it, buying it again from another class is wasted — you still attack twice, not three times. Further attacks come only from the Fighter’s higher steps (3 attacks at level 11, 4 at level 20), never from owning Extra Attack twice.

Premium features are the rare cases with no clean step to buy. Their numbers simply drift upward on their own (Rage's damage and daily uses, Wild Shape's available forms and CR). They also include the handful of always-on powers so strong the designers price them above an ordinary Passive. You buy these once, in the Premium band (section 9) — the dearest column on the Master Cost Table. One purchase covers all that future growth, but you pay a real premium up front.

The litmus test
Ask: does the upgrade have a name I would choose, or does it just quietly get bigger? A named higher version (2d6, a d8, a second attack, +15 ft) = re-buy each step at its own tier. A smooth numeric drift with no version to pick (Rage's damage bonus and uses, Wild Shape's CR) = buy once, in the Premium band, you pay up front for years of automatic growth. A few standout always-on features (Aura of Protection) also sit in Premium, not because they grow but because they are simply worth the premium price.
The exceptions: a Monk's Focus points and a Sorcerer's Sorcery Points are handled differently. Sorcery Points are bought on the per-point ladder too, with the first two free (then cumulative 0, 0, 1, 2, 4, 6) and the pool refreshing on a long rest. A Monk's Focus is bought the same way but with no free points — it starts at 1. For both, AP is the only limit, with no Hit-Dice gate.

A Barbarian buys Rage a single time, but it lands in the Premium band: T1 Premium, origin 12 AP (well over twice the price of an ordinary T1 Per-Rest feature). You do not re-buy Rage the way a rogue re-buys Sneak Attack, because Rage has no discrete named upgrades to choose between, instead you pay the Premium price once, and it covers every later improvement for free. That higher up-front cost is the price of never having to think about it again.

12. Innate Spellcasting

“Every spell is a debt. The wise pay it before they cast; the desperate, after.” — Archmagister Vel

Magic in PACT comes in two layers. The first is innate, and this chapter covers it: cantrips, the studied knacks that anyone — even a commoner — can learn and cast at will. They need no Foundation, no Rank and no slots. The second layer is the structured spellcasting that powers levelled spells, and it follows in the next chapter. Either way, magic bought at character generation is free of gold and time; in play it pays the ordinary section-16 gold and downtime, the same as any other purchase.

Cantrips

Cantrips escalate by count on a Steep curve. Each new cantrip costs (twice the number you already know from that Discipline) + 4 AP. So your 1st cantrip is 4 AP, your 2nd is 6, your 3rd is 8, and so on up the ladder below. You buy cantrips per Discipline, and each Discipline keeps its own count. The price ladder restarts at the base 4 AP for the first cantrip you take from any new Discipline's list.

The innate cantrips anyone can dabble in are drawn from the Wizard list and cast with Intelligence; the most you can hold this way equals your proficiency bonus + your Intelligence modifier. You don't need a Foundation to dabble. Anyone, even a commoner, may learn cantrips from the Wizard list, cast with Intelligence, up to that same cap. Cantrips are studied knacks, which is exactly why the open, learnable list is limited to the wizard's list — but levelled spells still need a Foundation.

Cantrips learned this way sit outside any Tradition of your own, so they never scale. Each rolls a single die, no matter your level or Rank (see Innate magic, section 12).

What a dabbler can and can't learn
Because the freely-learnable list is the Wizard list, a dabbler can pick up Fire Bolt, Mage Hand, Prestidigitation, Light, Ray of Frost, and the rest of the arcane staples. What they can't learn this way is magic that isn't studied, a warlock's Eldritch Blast (a patron's pact gift) or a priest's Sacred Flame and Guidance (a deity's grant). Those come only with the matching Discipline's Foundation: a future cleric who hasn't yet bought a Divine Foundation may dabble in wizard cantrips, but not yet in their own holy ones.

13. Spellcasters

Real spellcasting is built one piece at a time, each resting on the last. The idea is simple. You commit to a Tradition—a branch of magic—and your Spellcasting Rank in that Tradition sets how high your spells can reach across every Discipline it holds. Every spell you cast runs at that power. You can take more than one Tradition, but each is a separate engine you pay for in full, so becoming a true all-round caster takes heavy investment in every one. This chapter builds it in order: the Traditions and Disciplines you draw on, the Foundation that opens a Tradition, the Spellcasting Rank that sets your highest spell level, the slots that fuel your casting, and the spells you come to know.

The six words of spellcasting
Tradition, the broad wellspring of magic: Arcane, Divine, or Primal.
Discipline, one class's spell list inside a Tradition (the Wizard list, the Cleric list).
Foundation, the 7-AP purchase that opens a Discipline so you can cast levelled spells at all.
Rank, sets the highest spell level you may cast from a Tradition, across all of its Disciplines (Rank 3 = up to 3rd-level spells).
Slot, one casting; you spend it to cast and it returns on a long rest.
Spell known, a spell in your repertoire; buy or learn it once, then cast it with a slot.
Read them in order: a Foundation opens a Discipline; Rank sets how high you can reach; slots and known spells let you actually cast.

Spellcasting Foundation

To cast levelled spells at all you must buy a Spellcasting Foundation: a T1 purchase costing 7 AP per Tradition, available right at character creation. Your Foundation opens one Discipline—a single class's spell list—within that Tradition, and it lets you buy a Rank and slots for it. (The cantrip limit is the same with or without it; see above.)

Extra Discipline, same Tradition: adding a second, third, or fourth Discipline drawn from a Tradition you've already founded costs +7 / +14 / +21 AP. The extra Discipline shares that Tradition's Rank, but—like every spell list—needs its own spell slots (below).

A new Tradition requires its own 7-AP Foundation and its own Rank. Crossing from Arcane into Divine, say, is a fresh start: a second Foundation, a second Rank ladder, and a second slot pool.

Cantrips in your Tradition

A Foundation does more than open levelled spells. The cantrips you take from a Discipline in your own Tradition now scale with you, rolling more dice as your Hit Dice climb—exactly like a class caster's. They are priced the same as any cantrip: a base of 4 AP climbing on a Steep curve (+2 AP each), bought per Discipline. Spell slots, your casting capacity

The origin Discipline discount. If your origin class is a caster, every spell-economy purchase for its Discipline—Foundation, Rank, cantrips, slots, and individual spells—is discounted by a flat 1 AP per purchase, the same at every tier (floor 1). Magically Bound on that Discipline lowers each by a further 1 (floor 1). This is the one place the origin benefit reaches the spell economy: a non‑origin Discipline takes no discount — it pays the plain rate once you unlock its class, or a flat +1 per purchase until you do (the un‑unlocked surcharge, below) — and the universal ladders are never discounted.

Out of your Tradition. Only your origin Tradition takes the discount. Any other Tradition you found stands on its own Foundation and Rank and works exactly like your first: its spells and cantrips scale and upcast on that Tradition's Rank, just as your origin's do. The only difference is the price: a non-origin Tradition takes no origin discount, and until you unlock its class each purchase also pays the flat +1 un-unlocked surcharge — unlock the class and it settles to the plain full rate. A second Tradition is a whole second engine, powered the same but never on sale. More precisely, the +1 is keyed to the class, not the Tradition: any Discipline whose class you have not unlocked pays it — even one inside a Tradition you already cast in — and unlocking that class is what clears it.

Rank lets you reach a spell level and a known spell puts it in your repertoire, but neither lets you actually cast. For that you spend a spell slot. A slot is one casting, refreshed on a long rest, bought one at a time and tied to a spell level. As in core D&D a higher slot upcasts a lower spell: a 5th-level slot casts any spell you know of 5th level or lower. Cantrips never use a slot.

Granted spells — rituals and components. A spell you gain from a class, subclass, species, or item feature (an innate or always-prepared spell) may be cast as a Ritual if it has the Ritual tag. And every spell — granted or bought — still requires its full Verbal, Somatic, and Material components to cast.

A slot's price climbs with its level: 1st = 4, 2nd = 6, 3rd = 8, 4th = 10, 5th = 12, 6th = 14, 7th = 16, 8th = 18, 9th = 20 AP. Slots are bought per Discipline, confined to a single spell list, not shared. Each list you cast from has its own pool, and they carry no origin discount. You may only buy a slot of a level your Rank and Hit Dice already allow. If a spell you know appears on more than one of your lists, you may cast it from either Discipline's slots—your choice at the time.

There is a cap. Within a Discipline you may never own more slots of a given level than (a) 1 + your spellcasting ability modifier, and (b) the number you own at the next-lower level. Each Discipline's slots therefore form a descending staircase—wide at the bottom, narrow at the top, no taller than 1 + that list's casting modifier.

Spell slots versus spells known, don't confuse them
A spell known is a recipe in your book, you buy it once (or scribe it). A spell slot is a turn at the stove, you spend it to cast, and it returns each long rest. Every caster buys both, and how each class acquires its spells differs by tradition (Appendix D). The warlock is the one exception: its pact slots work differently, see its entry in Appendix D.

Spells known, your repertoire

By default you buy the spells you know one at a time. A spell costs half its spell level in AP, rounded up: 1st- and 2nd-level spells cost 1 AP, 3rd- and 4th-level cost 2 AP, 5th- and 6th-level cost 3 AP, 7th- and 8th-level cost 4 AP, and a 9th-level spell costs 5 AP. You can only buy spells of a level both your Rank and your Hit Dice allow. Not every class pays per spell: prepared casters (Cleric, Druid, Paladin) receive their whole list free with their tradition. Everyone else — the known casters and the wizard alike — buys each spell with AP. The number of spells you may know is capped at your spellcasting modifier + Hit Dice — one cap for known and prepared casters alike (prepared casters prepare that many each day; known casters keep that many learned). The wizard is the sole exception: its spellbook has no cap, so a wizard may learn as much of the Wizard list as it can pay for, one spell at a time. You may exceed a cap, but every spell past it costs double its normal AP.

Learning new spells

There are two ways to add a spell to your repertoire, and the choice is what keeps a wizard's famous breadth intact without taxing innate casters into the ground.

Buy it with AP (the default, open to every caster): pay half its spell level in AP (rounded up) and it is yours instantly, with no gold or downtime beyond the ordinary section-16 band. Fast and certain—but it competes with everything else for points. Any spell learnt this way must come from your known class list.

Scribe a found spell, the wizard's book (gold + downtime, no AP): this path belongs to the wizard alone. When the DM puts a scroll, a captured spellbook, or a willing teacher in front of a wizard, that wizard may copy the spell into its book for only its gold and downtime — no AP. It is DM-gated: it needs a source in the fiction, so it rewards exploration rather than replacing the AP price, and any spell scribed this way must come from the Wizard list. The other casters have no such path; they learn a spell by paying its AP or not at all.

Wizards vs. sorcerers and warlocks, kept fair
Every caster that doesn't get its list free buys spells with AP — the sorcerer, bard, warlock, and now the wizard too. What still sets the wizard apart is its book: alone among casters, it has no cap on spells known. Over a career it can learn its entire list one AP at a time, while the known casters stop at their modifier + Hit Dice. The wizard keeps one further privilege, straight from the core rules: when the DM hands it a scroll or a captured spellbook, it may scribe that spell at a reduced AP cost — gold and downtime stand in for a portion of the advancement points required — a treasure reward, not a standing discount. The known casters have no such path; their repertoire is learned in points or not at all. This keeps the wizard the broadest caster in the game without handing it free breadth the moment the gold economy is switched off.
Why magic feels expensive
A starting caster spends 7 (Foundation) + 4 (Rank 1) + a few cantrips and a first slot or two before swinging a weapon, easily half a level-1 budget. Individual spells known are cheap (half their level in AP), but the structure to cast them, the slots above all, is not. That is by design: in PACT, the licence to do magic should always cost more.

Because Rank is per Tradition and slots are per Discipline, multicasting has a clear, escalating price. A pure single-list caster pays one Foundation, one Rank, and one slot pool. Spell access is free of the class tax, but class features are not
Buying into a Discipline's spells never triggers the class economy: a Foundation opens a spell list directly, and you do not pay the section 11 class-unlock (7 × the classes you already own) just to cast from it. Nor does the +Tier feature surcharge ever touch a spellcasting purchase. Instead, the spell economy uses its own, gentler modifier: a flat ±1 AP per purchase, set by how you reach the Tradition. Your origin Tradition costs 1 less. A Tradition you have unlocked — or reach through an unlocked class — costs the plain price. A Tradition that is neither your origin nor unlocked costs 1 more. Foundation, Rank, slots, spells, and cantrips all take that ±1 (floor 1), never the +Tier.
The moment you want that class's non-spell features, the class tax applies. There are two ways to reach a cross-class feature (§11): unlock the class — 7 × the classes you already own — after which its features cost plain sticker; or skip the unlock and pay the cross-class surcharge (+Tier) on each feature you take. Unlocking pays off once you want several features from that class; the surcharge is cheaper for a one-off. So a Paladin can splash Warlock spells through a cheap Foundation, but to also take Eldritch Invocations (which are features) she must either unlock Warlock and buy them at sticker, or pay the surcharge on each.
In short: a Discipline buys the magic; the class unlock buys the toys. Keep them separate on your sheet.

Casting in armour

A common myth says spellcasters can't wear armour. They can—there is no arcane spell-failure rule in 2024 D&D. The only thing that stops you casting is wearing armour you are not proficient with (which also gives disadvantage on Strength and Dexterity rolls). Buy a category's proficiency in Appendix C and you cast freely in it: a wizard who pays the full 12 AP for heavy-armour proficiency casts in plate with no penalty at all.

Mage Armor vs. worn armour
Before you spend points on plate, remember the cheapest option of all. Mage Armor sets your AC to 13 + your Dexterity for a single 1st-level spell — with no Strength requirement, no stealth penalty, and no proficiency to buy — and it can be cast on an ally. It does not stack with worn armour (it only works while unarmoured). So Mage Armor is the caster's natural, almost-free defence; buying armour is the committed choice of a Strength-built war-mage, not the default. See the opportunity-cost note in Appendix C.

14. Boons, Drawbacks, and Bonds

“The gods give nothing freely, but their bargains are cheaper than a wizard's.” — Sister Ana of the Open Hand

Boons grant an advantage in exchange for a story-shaped string. Drawbacks do the reverse: you accept a hindrance and pocket extra AP for it. You take both at character creation, and the DM has the final say on which ones fit your table. Boons and Drawbacks are a significant extension of the core rules. They suit a points-buy game well, but they are wholly optional — a DM may disallow every boon and drawback and run PACT without them.

Feats: Arts & Techniques

Arts & Techniques are trained powers drawn from the 2024 PHB feat list — studied fighting methods, culinary skills, hard-won survival instincts, and specialist techniques. Each is individually priced on its own merits after stripping out any ability-score increase (buy that on the ability ladder, §5) and any skill or tool proficiency grant (buy those on the skill ladder, §7).

How they work: an Art or Technique is a one-time purchase with no escalating ladder and no slots. You can buy any entry whenever you meet its prerequisite. Some require a minimum ability score, a specific PACT feature, or a narrative condition the DM must approve. Most have no prerequisite.

Why PACT replaced feats. The 2024 feat system bundles things PACT sells separately: an ability-score increase, a skill or tool grant, sometimes a spell, and a special power — all packaged into one choice taken at a fixed level. A feat that grants +2 Intelligence and the Arcana skill forces you to take the whole bundle to get any single piece, locked to a fixed level — exactly the rigidity PACT removes by selling each part on its own ladder, when and if you want it. So PACT strips each feat down to its core active or passive power, removes the bundled extras, and prices what remains on its honest merits. The result is Arts and Techniques: the same powers the 2024 PHB offers, sold individually, with no level gates and no feat slots.

Tier reflects availability: powers available at character creation are Tier 1. Powers available from level 4 onwards with no chain requirement are Tier 3. With the even-only ability ladder (§5), all score minimums are even numbers (12, 14, 16).

Boons

Boons are optional extra abilities bought with AP — at creation or, with the DM's leave, in play. Each is a flat benefit, priced like any feature. Boons carry no story string — unlike the two Bonds later in this chapter — so you simply pay the points. A DM may add, re-price, gate, or veto any boon. They are grouped by theme below.

Why PACT adds Boons. PACT prices every class feature individually — but not every useful power belongs to a class. Innate toughness, a born athlete's edge, or a survivor's hard-won awareness: none of these sit cleanly on any class's feature list. Boons fill that gap. They are available to any character regardless of class, cost AP like any other feature, and carry no story string (unlike Bonds later in this chapter). A DM who wants a tighter game may disallow all Boons — the rest of PACT runs without them.

Drawbacks, bonus AP at character creation

Each drawback below grants AP up front. You can buy a drawback off later for three times the AP it granted — so a +3 AP drawback costs 9 AP to remove. Buying it off also needs your DM's approval and usually a story arc to justify it. Like any in-play purchase, it costs gold and downtime, read off the section 16 band. The tripled price is deliberate: it stops a drawback being a cheap loan you take when AP is scarce, then shrug off the moment you're rich. Drawbacks marked permanent lock away an ability for good and cannot be bought off at any price, so choose them carefully. No character may gain more than 12 AP total from drawbacks. The HP-reduction drawbacks, Frail and Glass Frame, do not stack with each other; nor can you offset them by buying Grit or Vigor at creation — no selling hit points for AP, then buying them back cheaply. You can also buy off a drawback later: spend AP equal to what you gained from it, and the drawback ends — including any stat cap it imposed. The DM must approve the removal and the narrative reason. Some drawbacks (Soul Debt, Prophesied Doom, Two Souls) are permanent story threads and should only lift when their arc resolves.

Stat caps. Some drawbacks impose a permanent ceiling on a specific ability score. That score can never be raised above the cap for as long as the drawback stands. Because a cap creates an immediate contradiction if you already exceed it, you may only take a capped drawback if your current score is at or below the cap. The cap is the price of entry — a character with WIS 12 cannot take a WIS ≤10 drawback until they have reduced their Wisdom to 10.

Every drawback needs your DM's approval
A drawback is a promise about how your character will be played, and the DM approves the choice, not just the drawback. Before any drawback grants AP, your DM signs off on three things:
1. It will actually bite. The DM rejects any drawback whose restriction your build would never feel, you can't take Ritual-Blind as a non-caster, or cripple an ability you never use, for free points. It has to cost your character something real.
2. The count is sane. The DM limits how many you stack, a good rule of thumb is two or three at most, so character creation doesn't become an AP farm.
3. You'll honour it in play. A drawback you collect and forget is worth nothing; the table holds you to its sting, and the DM may revoke the AP (or the drawback) if you don't live with it.

Bonds, magical and martial

The two options below are bonds, not boons. Like a boon they cost no AP — in fact each pays you 2 AP and discounts the power or training it covers — but in return your ability is sworn to a source you must keep faith with. Break that faith and you pay in access and standing, not in points. Treat them as their own category: a deal struck with someone who lent you what you can do.

❧ The Bargain
A young paladin came to Sister Ana proud of his oath, certain the power in his hands was simply his.
“And when it fails you?” she asked. “It will not.” She smiled the way the old do at the young.
“It will, on the day you most need it, and on that day you will learn what every bound soul learns: the strength was never a gift. It was a loan. Break it, and you repay it with interest, in the one currency no ledger can count.”

Magically Bound (+2 AP, a gain)

Magically Bound cannot be bought off. Unlike a drawback, you cannot simply spend AP to remove this binding. It is lifted only by the DM's agreement and a story — atonement, a quest, a severing of the pact. A whole campaign may be built around freeing a Magically Bound character from what lent them their power.

Magically Bound doesn't cost you AP — it pays you. Taking it grants 2 AP at character creation, exactly like a drawback, because you are accepting a real string on your magic. When you take it, choose one Discipline you can access. From then on, every spell-economy purchase for that Discipline — Foundation, Rank, cantrips, slots, and individual spells — costs 1 AP less (floor 1 each). It stacks with the origin Discipline discount (section 11): bind your origin class's own Discipline and the two combine for a flat 2 AP (floor 1). So this bond hands you 2 AP up front and quietly shaves a point (or more) off every magical purchase for as long as you keep faith — a saving that grows with every casting you buy.

A DM's tool, not a default. Magically Bound exists at a table only if the DM agrees it fits the campaign. Because it is a story binding as much as a discount, the DM decides whether it is available at all, and may restrict it to particular Disciplines or character types. In an official Dragonlance campaign, for instance, a DM might allow it only for mages and paladins — binding their magic to the gods or the Orders of High Sorcery — and forbid it for everyone else. Treat the 2 AP and the discount as something you negotiate with your DM, never something you simply take.

If you can access multiple Disciplines, you may take Magically Bound separately for each — once per spell list. Each binding grants its own 2 AP, its own −1 discount on that Discipline's spells, cantrips, and slots, and its own string.

What you are bound to

Magic, in PACT, is never wholly your own — it is lent. When you take Magically Bound you name the source that lends it, and you swear to keep faith with that source. The source can be almost anything that might plausibly grant power in exchange for loyalty:

A god and its church — the classic case. A cleric or paladin draws divine magic in return for upholding the faith: attending rites, tithing a share of treasure, running the church's errands, and living by its tenets.

An angelic or demonic patron, or a cult — a celestial choir, an archfiend, an old fey lord, a Great Old One. The power is real and often generous, but the patron's demands are stranger and sharper: a warlock might owe souls, secrets, sacrifices, or service on a schedule not of their choosing.

A guild, order, or academy — an arcane college, a mages' guild, a monastic tradition, a circle of druids. Here the binding is institutional rather than holy: you keep the order's rules, guard its secrets, pay its dues, and answer when it calls. Break with the institution and it stops teaching — and may want its lessons back.

A place, pact, or bloodline — a sacred spring, a bound spirit, an ancestral oath, a living grimoire. The string is whatever that source asks: return to the spring each season, feed the spirit, honour the oath.

When you don't behave

The discount and the free AP are the source's advance on your loyalty. Stop honouring the string — skip the tithes, ignore the summons, betray the tenets, sell the guild's secrets — and the source begins to call the loan in. This is gradual and dramatic, not a single switch, and the DM sets the pace:

First, a warning. Spells fizzle at dramatic moments. The source sends an omen, a dream, an irritated messenger. You feel the well running shallow.

Then, withdrawal. You steadily lose access to the bound Discipline's spells, list by list, highest-level first — the most powerful gifts are recalled before the cheap ones. Cantrips may linger longest.

Finally, severance. Persist in the betrayal and the Discipline goes fully dormant: no new spells, no casting from that list, until you atone — a quest, a penance, a restitution the DM judges sufficient. A patron scorned may also simply take back what it lent, with interest.

None of this touches a Discipline you bought normally without binding it. Only the magic you accepted on loan answers to a string. Choosing Magically Bound is therefore a roleplay promise as much as a discount: take it only if you intend to play the relationship.

Magically Bound in play
A faithful, obedient priest who binds the Cleric Discipline pays 1 AP less for every prayer, cantrip, and spell slot, and pockets 2 AP up front. It is a modest cut on any single purchase, but it compounds: a caster with a deep list and a wide spread of slots saves a point on each one, so over a career the obedient cleric is meaningfully the cheaper caster. But stray from the doctrine and the well runs dry: first your highest prayers go unanswered, then the whole list, until you make amends. The cheaper your magic, the heavier the leash.

For nine fully developed example organisations to bind to — their histories, reach, and the demands they make as you grow in power — see Appendix F.

Martially Bound (+2 AP, a gain)

Martially Bound is the warrior's pact. Where a caster swears their magic to a source, a warrior swears their training to a master, an order, or a cause. Taking it grants 2 AP at character creation, just like a drawback, because you are accepting a real string on your craft. Choose one class you can access. From then on every non-spell purchase that class trains you in costs 1 AP less (floor 1 each): the class's own features, plus the fighting styles, weapon masteries, and armour proficiency you learn through it. Feats and spellcasting are never touched — they are your own, not the order's.

It stacks with the origin discount. Bind your origin class and the two combine, so binding your home class is the deepest cut. The saving is smaller than a caster's Magically Bound — a martial's kit has fewer moving parts than the spell economy — and that is the point: a lighter gain for a lighter binding.

A lighter binding. No otherworldly power lends a warrior their skill — only discipline and loyalty — so the obligation is gentler than a magical pact. It is still the DM's to allow, and the DM may limit which orders or classes are open. But where a bound mage risks their very magic, a bound warrior risks only their place: break faith with your master or order and you lose the discount until you make amends, train anew, or pledge to another. There is no quest to sever the binding and no power reclaiming its gift — just a soldier who must earn back their standing.

What a warrior is bound to

Where a mage names a god or a patron, a warrior names a teacher or an institution, and the bond is mundane, not magical. Choose one when you take the bond: a master or mentor (the veteran who drilled you, owed respect and a good name carried into the world); an order, company, or watch (whose rules you keep, colours you wear, and call you answer); a school or martial tradition (whose techniques you guard and pass on); or a liege or a cause you are sworn to. There is no otherworldly creditor and no soul on the line, so the strangest, sharpest sources — an archfiend, a Great Old One — never lend a sword its edge. A warrior's bonds are human ones.

The longer they taught you, the worse betrayal sits. A novice who leaves early is barely missed, so breaking faith at low Hit Dice costs only the discount until you make amends, train anew, or pledge elsewhere. But a veteran who has taken everything an order can teach and then walked away is a traitor in their eyes, and the more they poured into you the more bitterly they resent the loss. At higher Hit Dice the order does not merely stop teaching: it withdraws your standing, closes its doors and its allies to you, and may send former comrades, rivals, or hunters to settle the score. It can never reclaim the skill already in your hands — the one mercy a scorned patron is not owed — but it can make the world a colder, more dangerous place for the one who left. The binding is light at first; by the time you are a master in your own right, it is not light at all.

15. Barred Options

“Every fence has a list of doors that don't open. This is mine.” — Hatch, retired fence

PACT's economy only works if you cannot side-step it. Anything from the core game that hands you a thing PACT makes you buy — for free — is either barred outright or converted into a normal purchase. The guiding principle:

❧ The Hole in the Boat
There is a story the ferryfolk tell. A man crossed the wide river each day and hated the toll, so one dawn he brought an auger and bored a small hole in the hull beneath his own bench, no wider than a thumb. “It troubles only me,” he reasoned, watching the river bead up through the wood. “My feet, my hole, my business.”

The one-line rule
If it gives you something PACT sells, you don't get it for free, you buy it, or you can't take it.

Arts & Techniques: Barred Entries

Some 2024 PHB feats hand out things PACT already sells on dedicated ladders (skills, saving throw proficiencies, hit points). Lucky and Inspiring Leader are allowed and bought on the feat ladder like any other feat.

Origin feats (2024). Of the new origin feats, Alert, Magic Initiate, Skilled, and Tough are barred outright — they appear in the list above. Each is little more than a free initiative edge, free spells, free skills, or free hit points, and PACT sells all of those. Crafter and Musician are allowed, but the tool proficiencies they grant follow the rule below. Healer, Lucky, Savage Attacker, and Tavern Brawler are allowed and bought on the feat ladder like any other feat.

Feats that grant a skill or tool. A few feats — Chef, Crafter, Musician, and the like — throw in a skill or tool proficiency on top of their main trick. That proficiency comes with the feat at no extra cost. Pricing every feat's small bonus on its own proved more trouble than it was worth, so the proficiency simply rides along with the feat that grants it. It still counts toward how many proficiencies you own, though, because skill and tool prices climb with the number you already have (section 7). During character creation, treat the free one as the last bought — after every proficiency you paid for — so it never inflates their cost.

Halfling's luck — reroll natural 1s — can be bought on its own, like any other species trait (section 10), even by a character who is not a halfling, at its listed cost. It comes built into the Halfling Heritage Pack, but it is not locked there. Anyone may buy the reroll and add it to their sheet.

Barred class features

Some class features simply grant a thing on PACT's price lists. These are removed as freebies. If you want the effect, you buy the underlying thing:

Jack of All Trades (Bard), and any 'half-proficiency to all checks' ability, is barred. Buy the skills you want on the skill ladder.

Any feature that grants free saving-throw proficiencies beyond your first is converted. Buy them on the saves ladder.

Any feature that grants free skills, tools, or expertise at level-up, converted: those become normal skill/Expertise purchases.

Barred species traits

Species traits that grant spells, skills, or expertise follow the same rule. The spell or skill must be bought through its normal ladder (section 10). Purely flavour or minor utility traits — darkvision, a swim speed — are bought once off the Master Cost Table as usual.

The DM's final say: restricting and gating any purchase

“A price tag is not a promise. Some doors open only to those who have walked far enough to knock.” — Maela Trune, wandering loremaster

Every price list in this book assumes a purchase is simply available to anyone with the AP. That assumption is the DM's to revoke. The Master Cost Table tells you what something costs — it never guarantees that your table, your campaign, or the fiction will let you buy it. The DM is the final gatekeeper and may restrict, re-price, or gate any ability as they see fit.

Flat restriction. A DM may rule an option simply unavailable in their world — no resurrection magic, no firearm masteries, a particular subclass that doesn't exist here. If it isn't in the campaign, no amount of AP buys it.

Story-gating. More often, an ability isn't barred — it's locked behind the fiction. This is the natural home of the mightiest powers: a 9th-level spell, a T7 capstone, a legendary technique. The DM may declare that the knowledge exists in only one place — held by one teacher or sealed in one tome — and that you must quest to find it before the purchase becomes available. Until you complete that story, the AP price alone won't open the door. Once you do, you pay the cost as normal.

Earned discounts. The same fiction that gates a power can also cheapen it. A quest that unlocks a high spell or capstone may, at the DM's discretion, also reduce its cost — this is exactly the Mentors and Specialists rule in section 16 working hand in hand with gating.

Gating the mightiest magic
PACT's economy makes a 9th-level spell affordable surprisingly early for a wealthy character, which is precisely why a DM may want to gate it behind a story instead of a price tag. A spell like Wish or True Resurrection might require a pilgrimage to its last surviving copy, an audience with the archmage who authored it, or a trial only the worthy survive. The Hit-Dice and Rank gates (sections 4 and 12) set the mechanical floor; story-gating lets the DM set a narrative one on top. You still pay the AP, but only once the tale has earned you the right to spend it.

What gold and time actually represent

Downtime is not idle time. Between adventures a character might spend weeks at strange work: sparring daily with a retired duelling-master who charges by the bruise; walking forest paths at dawn, burning druidic incense until a wild shape stops feeling like a trick and starts feeling like breathing; or tracking down an obscure fiend-lore scholar and spending six weeks in a cold archive translating texts that keep rearranging themselves. The specifics are as varied and strange as the ability being learned — the downtime cost is simply the slice of calendar it takes up.

What gold actually represents

Gold spent on advancement is not coins poured into a void. It covers everything the training actually requires:

Training fees — the master who will not teach just anyone, the retired soldier who charges by the bruise, the hedge-wizard with a waiting list.

Consumables — healing potions burned through during gruelling conditioning, alchemical reagents, medicinal herbs and liniment, ritual incense that smells like rot and apparently works.

Materials — practice weapons replaced, copying fees at a scriptorium, rare spell components a new cantrip needs to take root, the scroll that explains the final missing piece.

Access — a bribe to enter a restricted archive, travel costs to find the one person who knows, a donation to the forest shrine the party suspects is mostly whisky.

Upkeep — lodging near the training site, food when the work is too exhausting to forage.

The DM doesn't need to itemise these line by line — but the fiction is richer when they do.

What it looks like at the table

Martial — Extra Attack, a T4 ability, roughly six weeks and 350 gp at Standard pace: "Doran spends the winter camped outside Ironwall, drilling with Sergeant Maren — a legionnaire who survived three campaigns and now charges by the bruise. The 350 gold covers her fee, three practice swords he breaks, the leech-woman after the polearm incident, and lodging near the barracks. By spring, the sword arm knows what to do before the brain does."

Magical — Wild Shape, a T2 Premium feature, roughly six weeks and 350 gp: "Evara walks the forest paths at dawn for six weeks, burning through the druidic incense Grandmother Moss insists is non-negotiable. She sits with badgers. She watches deer. On the fortieth morning she reaches for the shape and it unfolds. The gold went to Grandmother Moss, three wax-sealed jars of reagents, and a donation to the forest shrine."

Downtime and the rest of life

Players do not have to spend every stretch of downtime on advancement. Casual activities — which consume time but not AP — include carousing and making contacts, managing a business or tending a Bastion, recovering from injury, researching a mystery without buying AP, or simply existing in the world. The trade-off is real: three months of downtime spent carousing for two leaves only one for training.

Mentors, specialists, and rare resources

“Anyone can swing a sword. It takes a killer to teach you where to put it.” — the Whispering School

Not every advantage is bought at a list price. The world holds exceptional teachers, hidden manuals, sacred springs, and master artisans. Finding one can reduce the cost of a specific ability — entirely at the DM's discretion.

A mundane trainer — the village swordmaster, a hedge-priest — changes nothing. You could learn that anywhere.

An exceptional mentor or resource is different — tracking down a legendary assassin to teach you Sneak Attack, studying a dead archmage's spellbook, bathing in a font of vigour. Each may grant a discount on that one purchase, or unlock something normally gated.

Treat these as adventure rewards, not a shopping option. The discount is whatever the DM judges the quest was worth — often a few AP, occasionally a waived gold or downtime cost. The point is to make training a story, not a transaction.

Trading coin for time

Coin and downtime are not locked together. With the DM’s leave, a specialist can pour one into the other — handy when a build is rich in one and starved of the other. The rate is steep on purpose: a convenience, never a shortcut. Pay roughly three times the gold to halve the downtime a purchase demands, or accept triple the downtime to cut its gold cost by half.

Reach for this only when the fiction supports it. A party flush with treasure but racing a ticking clock can buy back precious weeks for a heavy purse. A party with time to spare but empty pockets can train through a long winter instead of paying a master’s fee. The DM sets the exact exchange, and may refuse it outright when no plausible mentor, library, or workshop is at hand. The trade still has to make sense in the world.

Fast track, for campaigns that level quickly

Some campaigns race up the levels with barely a night’s rest between adventures. When weeks and months of in-world time simply aren’t on the table, the DM may swap the standard band for this compressed one. More of the early purchases are free. The first downtime cost is measured in days rather than a week. The gold figures are roughly halved, and the longest waits shrink from years to a few months. The rule is otherwise identical — in-play purchases still read their cost off the band, and the surcharges below still apply. Only the thresholds move. Which band fits your campaign — or none at all

The gold-and-time rule is entirely optional, and it has three settings, not two.

Off. The simplest option is to ignore gold and downtime altogether and let AP alone govern advancement. For many tables this is the right call. Tracking coin and a downtime calendar can get in the way of the story, slow the pace, and turn a dramatic moment into bookkeeping. If the economy is fighting your game rather than serving it, switch it off — nothing else in PACT depends on it.

If you do keep it, pick the band that matches your campaign's rhythm, because the standard band assumes a calendar. Its bigger purchases cost weeks, months, sometimes years of downtime. That works only if the campaign actually hands players those quiet stretches to train, craft, and recover. Many don't. A horror campaign may trap the party with nowhere safe to rest. A war keeps them on an active front. A doom-clock campaign runs a countdown that never pauses. In any of these, a hero wants a mid-tier purchase and is told it costs three months of downtime. The campaign never grants three months, and advancement quietly stalls. That is what the Fast track is for — early purchases free, the first cost a day or two, the steepest waits months rather than years.

The opposite kind of campaign is the sandbox or exploration game — built around long overland travel, passing seasons, and stretches of rest between expeditions. It gives players exactly the calendar the Standard band wants. There the downtime costs land naturally, and the Fast track would only hand things over too cheaply.

Where gold and time shine: Bastions. The economy pays off best when paired with strongholds (section 7). A tower, sanctum, or forge is exactly the kind of thing a hero should pour coin and weeks into. A Bastion bought and grown through gold and downtime feels earned and rooted in the fiction, instead of appearing from nowhere. If your table enjoys building and holding a base, keep the economy on for that reason alone.

Pick by the campaign's rhythm:

Off — when coin and calendar get in the way of the story.

Fast — war, horror, survival, or any campaign on a ticking clock, where downtime is scarce.

Standard — sandbox, exploration, and slow-burn home games, where weeks and months pass freely (and the natural home for Bastion-building).

Choose one at the start and hold to it for the whole campaign. Don't switch mid-game, or a purchase's price will move under the players' feet.

Low tiers are nearly free, on purpose
Tier 1 advances (1–2 AP) cost no gold and no downtime at all, and the next band is just a token 25 gp and a single week. Low-level characters should rocket up the early curve; the brakes of gold and, above all, time only really bite from the mid-tiers onward, where a single purchase can cost half a year of downtime or more.
Training two unlike things at once (DM's call)
Downtime costs normally stack, train two things and you spend both stretches of time back to back. But depending on what you're buying, your DM may let you pursue purchases concurrently, so their downtimes overlap instead of adding up. The test is whether the two efforts draw on different muscles. Studying a new spell (mind, library, quiet) and drilling a weapon mastery or martial technique (body, training yard) are unrelated enough that a disciplined hero can plausibly do both in the same months. When that’s true, the DM may charge only the longer of the two downtimes, not the sum.
Two efforts of the same kind, learning two spells, or grinding two martial techniques, compete for the same hours and focus, so they normally don't overlap; you train one, then the other. Gold is never shared this way: each purchase always pays its own coin. As always, the DM has the final say on what may run in parallel.

Starting wealth

Everything you buy at creation is free of gold, so a new hero needs a separate way to afford armour, a weapon, rations, and a few coins. PACT handles this with a small purse plus an optional AP-to-gold conversion:

Every character begins with a free purse of 25 gp for basic kit.

You may convert AP into starting gold at 1 AP = 50 gp, spent only on starting gear and coin.

Suggested cap: 8 AP (400 gp) at creation, so no one trades their whole build away for a pile of treasure.

Worked starting-gold example
Bryn the Fighter wants chain mail (75 gp) and a greatsword (50 gp), about 125 gp. His free purse covers 15; he spends 3 AP to convert 150 gp (3 × 50 gp), leaving a little for rations and rope. That 3 AP comes out of his 50-AP build budget like any other purchase.

Awarding AP

AP is the pulse of advancement, and you set its rhythm. You can drip it out session by session, or hand it in lumps at the milestones that matter, a villain felled, a vault opened, a city saved. Both work; choose whichever you will actually keep up with.

Typical AP between levels

The benchmark gaps are uneven on purpose. The early game (1→5) is generous, so new players taste a few toys quickly. The climb steepens through the upper-middle game (8→12), the costliest stretch, because that is where the big spell tiers, the second attacks, and the auras come online, and you want those to feel earned. The high game (12→20) flattens again: by then a hero’s identity is set, and AP mostly deepens what they already are. If a player feels stuck, check which band they are in before you blame the rules, a level-9 caster saving for 5th-level slots is meant to feel the pinch.

Story-paced advancement, and the fast first levels

Most published campaigns don't track experience points at all. They level on milestones: when the party clears a chapter or hits a story beat, everyone gains a level. Advancement is pinned to the plot, not to a counter, and a “level” no longer maps to a tidy amount of game time, one might take a single session, the next might take five.

Those same campaigns tend to front-load the early levels. The first few arrive fast, sometimes alarmingly so: Storm King's Thunder rushes a party to 5th level in its opening (one table reported three level-ups in a single night), and Dragonlance bumps you to 2nd almost before the ink is dry. The early game is meant to move; the system should keep up without lurching.

This is why PACT's default is to award AP by the session, not by the level (see the per-session pace above, around 7 AP per session). A per-session drip flows smoothly no matter how the story doles out levels: when the campaign throws three quick levels at the table, players aren't handed one enormous pile of points to spend at once, and when the story slows, the trickle simply keeps going. If you'd rather award the whole per-level lump at each milestone, use the cumulative budget in section 17 and hand over the difference, but expect the early levels to deliver large, lumpy awards.

There is a deeper reason to favour the drip, and it is one of the goals PACT is built around. Gradual progression is simply more fun. A steady trickle of points lets a player buy one hard-won improvement at a time, a new skill this week, a point of Vigor the next, and feel the weight of each choice. That slow accumulation is where investment in a character is born: a hero assembled piece by piece, each purchase earned and deliberated over, means far more to its player than one delivered in sudden lumps.

And because every advantage in PACT is bought the same way, you can take this as far as you like, the whole character can be built up gradually, not just the trimmings. A DM after maximum investment can start players with only part of their build and let the rest be earned in play, so even a hero's foundations arrive a piece at a time. Players who assemble their character bit by bit, rather than receiving big power-ups in sudden jumps, tend to care far more about each thing they own, because they remember earning it. Big, infrequent leaps spend that feeling all at once and leave the levels between them flat; whenever you can, favour the many small steps over the few large ones.

And remember that you hold the dial. The DM can waive or reduce any cost at any time, AP, gold, or downtime, to reward a clever play, ease a player past a rough patch, or simply keep the story moving. The numbers in this book are a default to lean on, never a cage; when the cost is getting in the way of the game, set it aside.

Rule of thumb: tie AP to time at the table (sessions), and let the campaign tie levels to the story. The two don't need to march in step.

Approving a character

When a player slides a finished sheet across the table, you are the last reader of the ledger. You are not hunting for cheats so much as making sure the character they built is the one they meant to build, and one the rest of the table can adventure beside.

Start with the total: it should sit at or under the budget you agreed, with no creative accounting. Then read the stat line. A hero who dumped a stat or two for points is playing the game exactly as intended. A hero who farmed every dump to the floor for a great heap of AP has stopped building a person and started mining the system — and the +20 ceiling on dumping is your quiet reminder of where that line lies. Look next at how they bought their toughness: Vigor and its kin are gated for a reason, and the arithmetic should hold.

The thing most worth your eye is magic. A caster who has poured a whole budget into slots and Rank can eclipse the party without ever looking broken on paper, keep one eye on the spell economy, always. Beyond that, a second class or a second origin is a real commitment, not a free splash; make sure it serves the concept and not merely the optimiser. And every drawback on the sheet is a promise: confirm the player truly intends to play the string they took the points for.

None of this is a checklist to tick. It is a conversation. If something gives you pause, say so, a minute at session zero saves an hour at the table.

Be careful starting above level 1

PACT assumes a hero begins as a single Hit Die and grows. Start a campaign higher, a larger opening budget, more Hit Dice out of the gate, and a quiet distortion creeps in.

The culprit is the creation-only purchases. A second origin class (14 AP), a second origin race, stat dumps, drawbacks, none can ever be bought once play begins, and the cross-class surcharge they sidestep is permanent. The more AP a hero starts with, the more these stop being options and become obligations: at a 90-AP start, a second origin class is simply the correct buy for almost everyone, and you will watch the table converge on the same few openings.

So prefer to start at level 1. If you must begin higher — a campaign of seasoned heroes, say — handle the creation-only options in the open. Decide which are reasonable at your table — dumping stats for points, a second origin class, racial traits at their cheap creation price, drawbacks taken for AP — and tell every player which are allowed, so each can take them freely. The danger was never the options themselves; it is one build quietly mining a creation-only door the others don’t know is open. Put the same doors in front of everyone, and that danger is gone.

Dials you can turn

One Tradition, one Discipline. The fastest way to keep magic simple, and contained, is to cap every caster at a single Tradition, or even a single Discipline. The party loses some breadth; you gain a far quieter spell economy and far fewer rules questions.

Gold and downtime. The in-play gold and time costs are a brake for campaigns with room for them. If yours has no downtime, a breathless, ticking-clock game, or simply one where you would rather not keep the books, cut those costs back or drop them entirely. AP then becomes the only currency you track, and nothing else breaks.

Re-price anything. Raise the starting budget for a high-powered game; cap Hit Dice for a gritty one; ban a school of magic that does not fit your world; change a drawback’s payout if it feels off. The economy is robust, it bends to your table without snapping. The only rule of thumb: change a price, then watch a character or two get built against it before you commit.

Drawbacks can be handed down, at any time. A drawback need not be chosen at creation. A DM may assign one mid-campaign as the honest cost of what happens in play, and award the matching AP for it. Example: a rogue gambles with a fiend over a locked vault and loses, so the DM hands her the Soul Debt drawback on the spot, her contract is real now, and so are the points it pays. Used sparingly, this turns one bad night at the table into a permanent, story-earned mark on the character.

Why magic costs more

If you take one thing from this chapter, take this: magic is the most flexible and the most abusable thing at the table, and PACT prices it accordingly.

A spell can fly, teleport, charm a king, scry a foe, raise the dead, or simply end a fight before it starts, feats no amount of sword-skill can match. A martial buys a better swing; a caster buys a new answer to every problem. And the player who learns to work the spell economy, squeezing the most slots, the highest Rank, the broadest list from every point, can trivialise challenges a fighter must grind through. That is not a fault in your players; it is the nature of magic.

So the spell stack is dearer, and slots are gated behind your casting modifier. The magic premium is not a tax on flavour, it is the brake on the one resource that can genuinely warp a campaign. When you read a sheet, or balance an encounter, spend your attention on the caster’s spell list before the barbarian’s axe. The axe does what it says on the tin. The spell list does whatever the player is clever enough to make it do.

How to read the Status column
Buy, an ordinary class feature; pay the sticker (origin) price off the Master Cost Table. Every entry is a one-time purchase unless its Status reads Buy (stepped) — re-buy each named step — or Buy (per point); universal repeatables (skills, Hit Dice, masteries, slots, cantrips) live on their own ladders, not here.
Buy (Premium), a feature bought once in the dearest Premium band, either it grows on its own forever with no step (Rage, Wild Shape) or it's a standout always-on power (Aura of Protection). Never re-bought (section 11).
Buy (stepped), a feature with named discrete upgrades; re-buy each step at the tier it unlocks (e.g. Sneak Attack 1d6→2d6→3d6, a Bardic or Martial Arts die climbing the die chain).
Buy (per point), a pool grown one point at a time on an escalating ladder with no Hit-Dice gate (Focus).
Barred, the feature itself isn't sold; it grants something PACT already sells (spells, skills, expertise, saves), so you buy that underlying thing on its own ladder (section 16).
Barred, where nothing equivalent exists to buy, the feature is removed entirely and is simply unavailable.
Subclass features follow the same rules as their parent class's features: priced by Tier × Band, origin-discounted if the subclass belongs to your origin class, and any spell/skill/save grant routed through its ladder.

Buying a subclass: domains, patrons, circles, and the like.

A subclass, a Cleric’s Divine Domain, a Warlock’s Patron, a Druid’s Circle, a Sorcerer’s Origin, and so on, is not a single purchase. It is a small menu, and you buy only the pieces you want, each for its own AP:

The expanded spell list. A subclass’s expanded spell list is one purchase. The costing logic is to pay the normal per-spell cost (§13) for the spells it grants at levels 3 and 5; every spell it grants at higher levels is free. The spells are always prepared and never count against your limit, and the origin discount applies as usual. See §13, Subclass spells, for the full rule; the per-class prices are in the tables below.

Individual subclass abilities. Each named feature is priced by its Tier and Band on the Master Cost Table, exactly like a base-class feature, origin-discounted if the subclass belongs to your origin class, surcharged if it is cross-class. Buy one, several, or all of them.

So “taking the Life Domain” might mean buying its expanded spell list plus Preserve Life and nothing else, or every ability it offers. The per-class tables that follow list each subclass’s abilities with their prices, so you can pick à la carte.

Your first subclass is free, the rest are locked.

Each class you can build from gives you one subclass for free: pick it, and you may buy its expanded spell list and any of its abilities at their normal prices (origin discount if it is your origin class, cross-class surcharge if not). Every other subclass in that class is locked. To open another you pay a one-time Subclass Unlock; the abilities inside are then bought at those same normal prices, the unlock is the only extra cost, and your origin discount still applies.

Subclass Unlock (per class): every subclass beyond your first costs a flat 15 AP to open, however many you already have. (Opening all four subclasses of a class runs 45 AP.)

This is what stops cherry-picking. Reaching into a second subclass for a single signature ability costs 15 AP before you buy the ability itself, a bad trade for one toy, but a fair tax once you invest in three or four of that subclass’s features. Breadth is available; idle dabbling is not.

“Blood writes the first page; the ledger writes all the rest.” — the Ledger of Aptitudes

This appendix lists every species' traits with its price. Your Heritage Pack (section 10) bundles the race-defining basics cheaply; everything else is bought on the normal ladders, exactly like a class feature or spell.

How to read the table

In pack — “Yes” means the trait is included in the pack price; the Origin AP column shows what it's worth à la carte, but you don't pay it again. A “—” means it's bought separately, or routed to a ladder.

Origin AP / Cross-race AP — Origin is the price for your own species; a trait from a different species adds the +1 cross-race surcharge.

Tier / Band — sets both the price and the Hit Dice you need. A trait is cheapest at level 1 and dearer the higher its Tier (Draconic Flight is T4 — not available until 5 Hit Dice).

A ladder route — a trait marked for a skill, Vigor, or the spell economy isn't bought here; pay for the underlying thing on its own ladder.

Cantrips cost the full 4 AP but are cap-exempt — they don't count against your cantrip limit.

Locked cantrips. High Elf and Wood Elf cantrips are fixed to Prestidigitation and Druidcraft (no swapping for any Wizard or Druid cantrip), so a free racial pick can't fish out a strong off-list cantrip like Fire Bolt. Buy others on the normal cantrip ladder.

Appendix C: General Proficiencies

“Armour is a craft like any other, the smith forges it, but you must learn to wear it.” — the Ledger of Aptitudes

In PACT, gear is neutral ground. Armour and weapon proficiencies cost the same for every character regardless of class — no origin discount, no cross-class surcharge. What makes two heroes different is the features they pair that gear with, never the price of the gear itself. The one class-based price difference in the whole system is the origin discount on class features (section 11); a wizard may wear plate and a fighter may dabble in spells, and the gear costs each of them exactly what it costs anyone else. What makes two heroes different is the features they pair that gear with, never the price of the gear itself.

Armour proficiency

Wearing armour effectively is a proficiency you buy. Each category below is a flat purchase, bought once, the same price for everyone, with no Tier and no Hit-Dice gate (it pays gold and downtime off the section 16 band only when bought in play).

Reminder, medium and heavy armour require Strength 10 for their AC to apply (see section 8). Light armour and shields do not.

How armour proficiency works
The categories stack. You buy each tier in order: a light-armour duellist pays only for Light, but heavier protection means owning the lighter tiers first. A shield stands alone and may be bought on its own.
The full climb. You must own each lighter category before buying a heavier one: Medium proficiency requires Light, and Heavy requires Medium (Shield stands alone). Full plate is therefore the whole climb, Light (2) + Medium (4) + Heavy (6) = 12 AP, exactly as all martial weapons build on Simple.
Wearing armour you aren't proficient with uses the core-rules penalty (disadvantage on Strength/Dexterity rolls, and no spellcasting). PACT doesn't change that, it just makes the proficiency something you choose and pay for.
Why armour has no Hit-Dice gate
Tiers gate purchases by level, a T3 feature needs 3 Hit Dice. Armour proficiency has no Tier, so that gate never applies: heavy armour is dearer, not later. A level-1 fighter can buy heavy-armour proficiency and wear chain mail from the first session, exactly as in the core rules. What actually paces plate is its 1,500 gp price and its Strength requirement, not a level lock, so it arrives when your wealth and your Strength do, not at a fixed Tier.
You can cast in armour you're proficient with
There is no arcane spell-failure rule in 2024 D&D, the myth that casters can't wear armour is just that, a myth. The only barrier is proficiency: wearing armour you lack training in blocks your spells, so a war-mage simply buys the proficiency and casts freely (section 13). Weigh it against Mage Armor first, though, AC 13 + Dexterity for one cheap spell, no Strength or stealth cost, and it doesn't stack with worn armour.

Weapon proficiency

“A blade you cannot hold true is just an expensive stick.” — Dorn, garrison weaponsmith

Basic weapon proficiency, adding your proficiency bonus to attacks with a weapon, is bought the same flat, class-neutral way, with no Tier and no gate. Untrained, you can still swing any weapon; you simply don't add your proficiency bonus until you've paid for it. Martial proficiency builds on Simple, and a weapon's mastery (section 8) can only be bought once you're proficient with that weapon.

Appendix D: How Each Caster Casts and Learns

Every caster pays for the same three things: a Foundation (access), a Spellcasting Rank (their ceiling), and spell slots (their fuel). You buy Foundation and Rank once per Tradition. You buy slots per Discipline—that is, per spell list. What changes between classes is how they build their repertoire, the spells they can actually cast. Magic is the most expensive path in PACT, so a full caster honestly costs more than a martial of the same level. That is by design, not a budget to balance away.

How many spells you may have is capped, and the cap depends on your tradition. Known casters cap at spellcasting modifier + Hit Dice (your level). Prepared casters—and the wizard's daily prepared count—cap at modifier + Hit Dice. You may go over your cap, but each spell past it costs double its normal AP.

Known casters, pay AP for every spell

Sorcerer, Bard, Warlock. A small, fixed set of spells, each bought with AP and kept for good — these casters have no scribing path, so their repertoire is learned in points or not at all. They cast spontaneously, with no preparation and nothing to lose, and lean on class tricks (Metamagic, Invocations, Bardic Inspiration). Known limit: modifier + Hit Dice (double cost to exceed). A Sorcerer’s Sorcery Points come bundled with buying into Sorcerer casting: you gain Sorcery Points equal to your Hit Dice, refreshed on a long rest — not bought point by point. Should you want more than your Hit Dice allow, extra points are bought one at a time on a steep ladder that starts at 3 AP and climbs — a rare splurge, not the standard path. Points fuel Metamagic, and you can spend them to conjure a spell slot of a level you can already cast. Cost per extra point on the Steep ladder from 3 — 1st 3, 2nd 5, 3rd 7, 4th 9, 5th 11, 6th 13; cumulative 3 / 8 / 15 / 24 / 35 / 48 (the Nth extra point costs 2N+1; N points total N(N+2)).

Prepared casters, the whole list, free

A subclass’s bonus spells are cheap and always prepared. You pay the normal per-spell cost (above) for the spells your subclass grants at level 3 and level 5 — and nothing more; every spell it grants at higher levels is free. A full-caster subclass therefore costs its first six spells (its 1st-, 2nd-, and 3rd-level grants); a half-caster’s, its first four. The origin discount applies as usual, since a subclass is almost always your origin class. The free higher-level spells are a reward for commitment: you cannot cast them until your Rank and Hit Dice reach their level, many levels after you buy the list. A subclass that also grants a cantrip charges a flat 4 AP for it (it never counts against your cantrip cap). Where a subclass grants spells through a higher-level feature instead of an expanded list — a Wizard’s Spell Breaker, a Bard’s Magical Discoveries — that feature is priced by its Tier and Band on the Master Cost Table, like any ability.

Cleric, Druid, Paladin, Ranger. The entire class list is theirs, with no AP per spell. Each day they prepare a limited number (modifier + Hit Dice) and may swap on a long rest. AP goes to Foundation, Rank, and slots only.

Spellbook caster, the uncapped list

Wizard. The wizard buys its spells with AP, exactly like a known caster, with one defining edge: its spellbook has no cap. Where a known caster stops at modifier + Hit Dice spells, a wizard may learn as much of the Wizard list as it can pay for, then prepares a daily subset (modifier + Hit Dice). AP goes to Foundation, Rank, slots, and spells. The classic spellbook survives as a treasure path: when the DM provides a scroll or a captured book, the wizard may scribe that spell at a reduced AP cost, with gold and downtime counting toward the purchase — and if the book is lost, re-scribing it at the same reduced cost restores the library.

Half-casters

Paladin and Ranger are half-casters. Their Rank caps at 5—never 6th-level spells or above—and they unlock spell levels later (2nd at level 5, 3rd at 9, 4th at 13, 5th at 17). They buy slots on the same à la carte curve as everyone else, simply capped at 5th level; there is no separate half-caster table. The Paladin and the Ranger are both prepared casters.

Worked example. A level-9 Paladin (Charisma +3) who wants 3rd-level smites pays the Divine Foundation (7 AP, or 6 after his Divine origin discount). He buys Spellcasting Rank up to 3 on the standard ladder, then buys slots à la carte — say three 1st, three 2nd, and two 3rd, capped at 5th level forever. Because he is a prepared caster, he readies a fresh handful each long rest (Charisma modifier + half his level) from the whole Paladin list, and never spends AP on individual spells.

Both half-casters prepare the same way. The Paladin and the Ranger each prepare from their full class list — Divine and Primal — and swap freely on a long rest, so their AP buys ceiling and fuel, not a spellbook. Both share the half-caster cap: Rank never climbs past 5, and the late unlocks (2nd at 5, 3rd at 9, 4th at 13, 5th at 17) keep a half-caster a tier or two behind a full caster of the same level. That lag is the point. They pay less and cast less, staying a martial first and a caster second. Neither gains cantrips. A half-caster's casting is levelled spells only — a Paladin or Ranger Discipline grants no cantrips at all, scaling or otherwise (matching the 2024 PHB). For a cantrip, dabble on the universal innate path (§12), as any non-caster may.

Sorcerers, innate fire and the Font of Magic

The Sorcerer is a known caster with a twist all its own: a pool of Sorcery Points (the Font of Magic) that fuels Metamagic and converts into extra slots. Like every caster, it buys a Foundation (7 AP per Tradition, Arcane by default), Spellcasting Rank on the standard ladder, slots per Discipline, and each spell individually with AP. What sets it apart is how it pays for its fuel.

Sorcery Points are bought one point at a time on a bands-of-two ladder starting at 0: your 1st–2nd cost 0 AP each, your 3rd–4th cost 1 each, your 5th–6th cost 2 each (cumulative 0, 0, 1, 2, 4, 6), and the pool refreshes on a long rest. The Monk's Focus is bought the same way (the Gentle bands-of-two ladder), except Focus has no free points — it starts at 1 (§15).

Everything else is bought like any other known caster. Innate Sorcery, Metamagic, Sorcerous Restoration and the rest are class features bought once at their tier price (Appendix A), and subclass spells flow through the normal spell economy. Metamagic in particular requires the Sorcerer Discipline (the Font of Magic): its Sorcery Points are what fuel it, so you cannot buy Metamagic without first founding the Sorcerer's Tradition.

The Warlock is its own thing

Pact slots. The warlock owns only a few slots, but every one casts at your highest available spell level (capped at 5th) and recovers on a short or long rest. A long rest always refills them, so an unreliable short rest never leaves you empty. You may own pact slots up to your Charisma modifier + proficiency bonus, bought on an escalating ladder: your Nth pact slot costs 3 + 3N AP (6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21…). There is no surcharge for the short-rest recovery as they are not priced like ordinary slots.

Subclass casters: Eldritch Knight and Arcane Trickster

A couple of 2024 subclasses—the Fighter’s Eldritch Knight and the Rogue’s Arcane Trickster—hand out a trickle of wizard spells as a subclass feature. PACT does not sell that feature, which is why you will see it marked “Barred → spell economy” in Appendix A. The casting is not barred; it is simply bought the way every caster buys magic.

A Fighter who wants spells founds the Arcane Tradition and takes the Wizard Discipline, then pays for the Foundation, Spellcasting Rank, slots, and spells on the usual ladders. Because the Wizard list is neither their origin nor an unlocked class, every one of those purchases pays the flat +1 un-unlocked surcharge on top — the +Tier feature surcharge never applies to spells, and unlocking the class drops the +1 to nothing. A Rogue who wants the Arcane Trickster’s tricks does exactly the same. The result is a spell-blade built from the same parts as any other caster, with no special case and no bespoke rule.

There is an upside, too. You are not boxed into the subclass’s short, fixed spell list—you cast from the whole Wizard Discipline, buying as many or as few spells as you like. The subclass flavour is yours for free; the magic behind it is simply on the menu like everything else.

Appendix F: Powers That Bind

“No magic is a gift. It is a loan, and every lender keeps a ledger.” — the Ledger of Aptitudes

A character who takes Magically Bound (section 14) swears their magic to a source that lends it. This appendix offers nine ready-to-use sources — a church, a cult, an academy, a grove, a knightly order, a host of judging angels, a bloodline ember, a broken muse, and a door onto nowhere. Each comes with its history, its reach, what it grants, and what it asks. Drop one in whole, or use them as patterns for your own.

A source lends power on credit. Its demands fall into five kinds: Devotion (regular observance), Tithe (a recurring price), Service (answering the call), Prohibition (what you must never do), and Sacrifice (a larger, one-off cost). The demands escalate. The entry obligations are only the interest — but as a bound character gains Hit Dice and draws ever more from the well, the source begins to collect on the principal. Each organisation below lists its demands in four bands keyed to Hit Dice: Initiate (1–4 HD), Sworn (5–9), Anointed (10–14), and Exalted (15+), growing from light observance to the duties of a high officer of the order. (For what happens when a bound character stops paying, see the consequence ladder in section 14.)

1. The Kindled Dawn

What it is: The largest faith in the civilised lands — a church of light, warmth, and renewal that worships Aurel the Dawnfather, the god said to relight the sun each morning. Its priests are healers and its paladins are dawn-knights. Its symbol — a rising sun over an open hand — hangs in market squares everywhere. (Binds clerics and paladins, Divine.)

Alignment: Lawful Good

History: Founded after the Sunless Year — a winter that would not end — when a shepherd-prophet named Selene carried the 'first ember' out of a buried shrine and the sun rose again. The church has stood ever since as a bulwark against darkness in every form. It remembers the Sunless Year in an annual vigil, where every flame in a city is doused and then relit at dawn.

Spread: Mainstream and vast — a chapel in nearly every town, soaring cathedrals in the cities, and missionary chapters pushing into the frontier. A cleric of the Dawn is never far from a brother or a bed.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Initiate (1–4 HD): Keep the dawn prayer; observe the holy days; carry the faith's light openly; speak a rite over the dead you make.

Sworn (5–9 HD): Tithe a tenth; destroy undeath where you find it; never abandon an innocent to the dark; minister where the church sends you.

Anointed (10–14 HD): Lead a congregation or chapter; the tithe rises to a quarter, or you fund and staff a chapel; a bishop assigns you a named darkness to break; the church's call comes before your own plans.

Exalted (15+ HD): Raised to high office — made a prelate of a see, handed a cathedral and a diocese to govern. You ordain priests, sit the synods, settle points of doctrine, and steward the church's lands and coffers. The church recalls you from the field to take your seat. Long stretches now go to administering the faithful, and the open road becomes something you snatch between duties.

2. The Maw

What it is: Not a god but an appetite — an ancient entity sealed in the deep places, older than names, that grants power to those who feed it. Its cult has no temples, only cells: marks scratched in cellars, and a hunger passed mouth to ear. (Binds warlocks, Arcane.)

Alignment: Neutral Evil

History: When the first cities were raised, something vast and starving was bound beneath them by powers now forgotten. It cannot break free, but it can bargain — and over the ages a thin, secret cult has formed around the trade: morsels for magic. Most who feed the Maw believe they are using it. The Maw believes otherwise, and the Maw is patient.

Spread: Hidden and thin — a handful of cells in the great cities, a corrupted village or two, a name you stumble into rather than seek. To outsiders it does not appear to exist at all.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Initiate (1–4 HD): Never speak its name; answer when the mark burns; offer a small morsel when it stirs — a coin of memory, a night's sleep.

Sworn (5–9 HD): Each new moon, offer a true morsel — a memory, a name, a year of someone's luck; carry its mark hidden.

Anointed (10–14 HD): Morsels grow to significant lives, or to pieces of you — a year off your span, the colour of your eyes, a feeling lost forever; open a new cell or corrupt someone who trusts you.

Exalted (15+ HD): Named its high steward — keeper of its sealed prison and master of its cells. You conduct the great rites, oversee and discipline lesser cultists, and manage the spread of its mark. You may not stray far or long from its place of power. The hunger no longer merely feeds on you; it puts you to work running its church of appetite, and that work fills your days.

3. The Argent Conclave

What it is: The pre-eminent college of arcane study — a citadel-library of silver towers, a faculty of archmages, and a jealous monopoly on rare knowledge. Its members are scholars, its debts are paid in spells, and its enemies are mostly other wizards. (Binds wizards, Arcane.)

Alignment: Lawful Neutral

History: Raised after the Unmaking — a magical catastrophe that erased a kingdom and half its libraries. Its founders swore to gather and guard all arcane knowledge, so that no such ruin could ever come of ignorance again. It was a noble charter — and over the centuries it has hardened into bureaucracy, rivalry, and a great deal of politics. The library beneath its towers is said to hold a copy of every spell ever properly scribed.

Spread: A central citadel, plus chapter-houses in the major cities, a web of alumni in courts and towers, and field agents called Reclaimers. Respected, semi-political, and quietly powerful.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Initiate (1–4 HD): Copy every spell you scribe into the vault; keep the Conclave's secrets; pay your dues.

Sworn (5–9 HD): Never teach outsiders or rivals; accept assignments — recover a lost tome, investigate rogue magic; attend the conclaves.

Anointed (10–14 HD): Take a committee chair, run a chapter-house, answer for apprentices; surrender your signature discoveries to the vault under the Conclave's name; take a side in its feuds.

Exalted (15+ HD): Elevated to the ruling council as an Archmagus. You keep residence at the citadel for long terms, chair its committees, manage its budgets, feuds, and apprentices, and oversee the vault. The seat is decades of duty that bind you to the silver towers. Adventuring becomes a rare leave of absence, not a life.

4. The Mothwood Circle

What it is: A circle of druids bound not to a god but to a place — the Mothwood, a vast old forest — and to the land-spirit that sleeps at its heart, the Pale Stag. The Circle's magic is the forest's own, lent on the understanding that the forest will, one day, ask for it back. (Binds druids, Primal.)

Alignment: True Neutral

History: The Mothwood is older than any kingdom, and the Circle older than any king. Its first wardens made a pact with the Pale Stag in an age when the wild and the tilled lands were still deciding their borders. That pact is renewed every generation at the Turning, when the Circle gathers in the grove to swear the old law again.

Spread: Rooted to one wild region, though its kin wander far — small, scattered, and secretive, recognised by other druids and almost no one else. There are perhaps a few dozen true Circle-kin at any time.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Initiate (1–4 HD): Return to the grove each season; take only what is needed, never the last of a kind.

Sworn (5–9 HD): Tend and defend the wild; keep a hard vow (never fell a living tree, never carry worked metal into the deep wood); mend the small hurts of the land.

Anointed (10–14 HD): A longer penance each season; a wound of the wild to mend over months; made warden of a stretch of forest, bound to defend it.

Exalted (15+ HD): Made an elder of the Circle and keeper of the grove. You lead the seasonal rites, govern the scattered circle-kin, settle their disputes, and train the initiates. You must remain near the wood through the long turnings. The land's stewardship roots you; wandering far or long is no longer yours to choose.

5. The Silver Watch

What it is: An austere order of dawn-knights and oath-sworn watchers, bound to a host of celestials led by the archon Vael, founded for a single purpose — to stand against the return of a sealed evil. Where the Kindled Dawn is a warm public faith, the Watch is a cold, vigilant brotherhood of fortress-monasteries on the dangerous edges of the map. (Binds paladins, clerics, and celestial-pact warlocks, Divine / celestial.)

Alignment: Lawful Good

History: Ages ago a great evil — remembered only as the Return — was bound at terrible cost, and a celestial host swore to watch the seal forever. Mortals were enlisted to keep the vigil the celestials could not keep alone, and so the Silver Watch was formed: knights who hold the border-fastnesses, hunt the Return's heralds, and never stand down.

Spread: Small and elite — a chain of border fortress-monasteries, a few hundred sworn members, revered by those who know them and unknown to most. They go where the seal is thin.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Initiate (1–4 HD): Keep the oath (never break a sworn word, never refuse sanctuary); live austere; stand the vigil when called.

Sworn (5–9 HD): Hunt the Return's heralds; report every sighting; keep no luxury and no excess.

Anointed (10–14 HD): A stretch of border to hold; a herald to hunt to its end; no leave to retire or settle while the Return stirs; austerity hardens towards poverty.

Exalted (15+ HD): Given command — made castellan of a fortress-monastery and a stretch of the border. You hold the wall, which means you garrison it: train and discipline the knights, keep the watch-rosters and supply lines, answer for the seal in your charge. The Watch expects you present at your post, not ranging from it; the campaign road narrows to the occasional sortie.

6. The Seraphic Edict

What it is: Not a god but a verdict — a host of nameless seraphim who serve a single law: that evil, wherever it is found, must be unmade. They lend their fire to mortals willing to become its instrument. (Binds clerics and paladins, Divine.)

Alignment: Lawful Neutral, in service of a Good cause

History: When the first darkness was driven back, the victors did not rest. They became the Edict — an order of burning judges sworn never to sheathe the sword while a single shadow remained. They do not forgive and they do not stop, and those they touch inherit both the fire and the refusal.

Spread: Known but feared — bright chapels in the great cities, a sigil of an open eye wreathed in flame, and a reputation that empties a tavern. Even the devout keep their distance.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Initiate (1–4 HD): Never knowingly let a cruelty pass unanswered; speak no lie to the innocent; keep both your blade and your conscience clean.

Sworn (5–9 HD): Hunt evil you could safely have ignored. Mercy to the wicked is itself a sin — you may not take a bribe, a truce, or an easy rest while a known evil works nearby.

Anointed (10–14 HD): Burn what cannot be redeemed, whatever it costs you — a cursed relic, a corrupted town, a friend who has turned. Hesitation is counted as heresy.

Exalted (15+ HD): Become a living verdict. You may never retire, never look away, and never weigh the price; the Edict will spend you to the last drop against the dark.

7. The Sleeping Flame

What it is: Not a patron you sought but a thing that woke inside you — an ancient ember of raw magic coiled in your blood, dreaming, stirring a little more each year. Your power is the sound of its breathing. (Binds sorcerers, any Tradition.)

Alignment: Chaotic Neutral

History: Somewhere up your line an ancestor swallowed a fire that should have killed them — a shard of a dragon, an elemental, a dying star; no one now agrees which. It has slept in the blood ever since, passing down quiet for generations, until it chose you to wake in. It is not cruel. It is only hungry to burn, and you are its lamp.

Spread: As rare as the bloodlines that carry it — a scattering of marked families, a birth-omen here, a scorched cradle there. Most who carry the Flame never know until it lights.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Stirring (1–4 HD): Let it breathe. You must use your magic often and openly; smother it too long and it wakes you with fevers and sparks you cannot control.

Waking (5–9 HD): Feed it spectacle. Once between long rests it craves a true expression of power — a spell cast at its fullest, a fire that leaves a mark — or it grows restless and your control slips.

Burning (10–14 HD): Its temper is yours now. In moments of strong feeling you must give it voice, loosing magic you did not quite choose; holding the surge back costs you dearly.

Ascendant (15+ HD): You and the ember are no longer separable. You may never go long without burning bright — and one day, not yet, it will want to wake the rest of the way.

8. The Riven Chord

What it is: Not a god but a note that will not resolve — a wandering muse-spirit of broken music that lends genius to whoever will carry its unfinished song towards its ending. (Binds bards, any Tradition.)

Alignment: Chaotic Good

History: It was once a single perfect work, shattered before its final chord was struck, its maker lost to silence. What remains drifts the world as a hunger for completion, settling on player after player and lending each a spark of the lost brilliance — in exchange for a few more bars of the song. None has yet finished it. The Chord does not mind; it has all the time in the world and a new voice every generation.

Spread: Half-legend — a tune that haunts a region for a season, a prodigy who appears from nowhere, a melody strangers find themselves humming. Bards know the stories; few believe them until the Chord finds them.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Novice (1–4 HD): Never repeat yourself. Each performance must carry something new — a fresh verse, an untried turn of phrase. Stale art starves the muse.

Voice (5–9 HD): Carry the song forward. Between adventures you must compose, perform to a true audience, and add to the unfinished work; a season of silence and the spark dims.

Virtuoso (10–14 HD): Spend yourself for the perfect moment. When the Chord swells you must answer it — improvise into danger, play when you should flee, take the brilliant risk over the safe retreat.

Maestro (15+ HD): You are the song’s best hope and its prisoner. You may never lay down your art, and you are bound to seek its final chord even knowing it may be the last note you ever play.

9. The Door That Wasn’t

What it is: Not a being, not a place — a wrongness; a door that opens onto a room which does not exist, and lends magic to anyone who will keep walking through it. No two who serve it describe it the same way. (Binds any caster, any Tradition.)

Alignment: Chaotic Neutral (it defies easy alignment)

History: It has no history — or rather too many, none agreeing. Scholars who study it find their notes rewritten; those who map it find the map is of somewhere else. The one constant is the bargain: step through, take the power waiting on the far side, and leave a little of your certainty behind. People have served the Door for centuries. The Door says it has never met a single one of them.

Spread: Everywhere and nowhere — a stair with one too many steps, a hallway longer going than coming back, a reflection that turns a beat late. You do not find the Door; it finds a use for you.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Threshold (1–4 HD): Keep an open mind, literally. Now and then you must do something genuinely strange at the Door’s prompting — take the wrong turn, answer a question no one asked — and never explain it.

Wanderer (5–9 HD): Trade certainty for power. Each time you draw deeply on the Door you forget one small true thing — a name, a face, a fact — and gain a small false one you cannot tell from the rest.

Untethered (10–14 HD): Go where it points. You must follow the Door’s pull into places that make no sense, trading safe plans for impossible ones; refuse it and your magic unmoors for a time.

Hinge (15+ HD): You are part of the threshold now, half here and half elsewhere. You may never fully trust your own memory again, and the Door may, at any time, ask you to hold it open while something else comes through.

Appendix G: Orders That Bind

A character who takes Martially Bound (section 14) swears their training to a master, an order, or a cause that drilled it into them. This appendix gives you eight ready-made orders — a free company, a war-lodge, a thieves' guild, a knightly order, a monastery, a fighting school, a ranger fellowship, and a lone master. Each comes with its history, its reach, and the duties it lays on you as you rise. Drop one in whole, or use them as patterns for your own.

An order trains you on credit, and its claim grows with your rank. The entry duty is light — a single standing obligation. But as a bound warrior gains Hit Dice and takes ever more of what the order can teach, it expects ever more in return — and resents betrayal all the more bitterly (section 14). Each order lists its demands in four bands keyed to Hit Dice: Recruit (1–4 HD), Veteran (5–9), Champion (10–14), and Master (15+). Unlike a Power, an order lends discipline, not magic. Breaking faith costs your standing, your rank, and your welcome — never your soul. There is no quest to sever it, only standing to win back.

1. The Iron Pact

What it is: A free company of sell-swords, centuries old. It sells its blades to the highest lawful bidder and keeps its word to the letter. (Binds any martial, especially Fighters.)

Alignment: Lawful Neutral

History: Forged from a disbanded royal army that refused to scatter, the Pact has outlived the kingdom that raised it. It keeps a ledger of every contract it has ever honoured — and it has never broken one.

Spread: International. It keeps chapter-houses in every free city, and a reputation that opens gates. Where there is a war, there is a Pact recruiter.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Recruit (1–4 HD): Answer the muster when the company calls. Never take a contract against the Pact. Turn over its share of your spoils.

Veteran (5–9 HD): Lead a lance of recruits. The company's share of your earnings rises. Vouch for the Pact's name, and settle its debts of honour.

Champion (10–14 HD): Hold a captaincy and train others. The company's contracts come before your own ventures.

Master (15+ HD): Sit the company council and set its contracts. The Pact expects you at the table, not the field. A captain who walks away takes its secrets and clients with them — and that, it will not forgive.

2. The Bear-Lodge

What it is: A roaming war-band sworn to the Great Bear and the old taboos. Its people hold strength to be the only law worth keeping. (Binds Barbarians.)

Alignment: Chaotic Neutral

History: As old as the northern snows, the lodge is passed hand to hand by trial rather than text. Its chiefs are remembered by the scars they earned, not the years they ruled.

Spread: Regional and nomadic. The lodge follows the herds and the wars. You find it by its fires, not its address.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Recruit (1–4 HD): Keep the lodge's taboos — never strike a sleeping foe, never refuse a rightful challenge — and bring meat to the fire.

Veteran (5–9 HD): Stand in the lodge's brawls and blood-feuds. Take a younger fighter under your arm. Defend the totem's ground.

Champion (10–14 HD): Speak for the lodge in challenges and lead its raids. Your reputation is the lodge's — and a coward's deed shames you both.

Master (15+ HD): Become a totem-bearer — a living standard of the band. They look to you in every fight. To abandon them now is to break the totem itself, a betrayal the lodge avenges.

3. The Silent Hand

What it is: A guild of cutpurses, fences, and shadow-blades that runs the underside of a dozen cities. (Binds Rogues.)

Alignment: True Neutral

History: No founding anyone admits to. The Hand simply was, is, and will be — and it remembers every debt longer than any court.

Spread: Urban and far-reaching. There's a safe-house in every city worth robbing, and a sign chalked where the Hand holds sway.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Recruit (1–4 HD): Keep the guild's secrets. Tithe a tenth of every haul. Never betray a fellow of the Hand.

Veteran (5–9 HD): Run a crew. The tithe rises. Take the jobs the Hand assigns, and fence through its channels.

Champion (10–14 HD): Hold a district and settle disputes. The Hand's work comes first, and its enemies become yours.

Master (15+ HD): Sit among the guild's heads, hidden even from each other. You set the marks and the prices. A master who turns informer or freelances is hunted by the whole Hand — quietly, and for good.

4. The Order of the Unbroken Shield

What it is: A chivalric order sworn to shield the helpless and keep its word though the heavens fall. (Binds Fighters and Paladins.)

Alignment: Lawful Good

History: Founded by knights who survived a massacre they could not prevent and vowed never again. The order's shield is notched once for every battle it has stood.

Spread: Respected and widespread. It keeps commanderies along the trade roads, a chapter at most courts, and a name that opens honest doors.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Recruit (1–4 HD): Uphold the code, protect the weak, and keep your word. Answer your liege's summons.

Veteran (5–9 HD): Take a squire and hold a post on the order's watch. Never leave an innocent to harm while you can prevent it.

Champion (10–14 HD): Command a commandery and sit in judgement. The order's call and the realm's defence come before your own quests.

Master (15+ HD): Be raised Marshal, a pillar of the order. You set its campaigns and guard its honour. A Marshal who breaks faith is stripped, denounced, and made an example — for the order's name is its only treasure.

5. The Ninefold Path

What it is: A mountain monastery whose monks pursue perfection of body and will along nine ascending disciplines. (Binds Monks.)

Alignment: Lawful Neutral

History: Built around a spring said to still the mind, the Path has trained warrior-monks for a thousand years. It keeps no record but the living memory of its masters.

Spread: Remote but renowned. There is one great monastery and a scattering of hidden dojos. Its students are known by their stillness.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Recruit (1–4 HD): Keep the daily forms and a novice's vows. Return to the mountain each year. Do not shame the Path in anger.

Veteran (5–9 HD): Teach the lower forms and undertake a pilgrimage the masters set. Master your temper as well as your strikes.

Champion (10–14 HD): Hold a dojo and carry the Path's judgements into the world. The monastery's needs come before your wandering.

Master (15+ HD): Be named a master of a discipline, owed the training of the next generation. One who takes the highest forms and then leaves is held to have stolen them — and the Path sends its own to recover what was taught.

6. The Crimson Ring

What it is: A famed fighting school and circuit of pits where champions are made — and wagered on. (Binds any melee combatant.)

Alignment: Chaotic Neutral

History: Grown from a single arena into a circuit of them, the Ring sells spectacle. It trains its fighters to give the crowd its money's worth, and a champion's name is worth a fortune.

Spread: City to city, wherever crowds gather and coin flows. The Ring's red banner means blood and silver.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Recruit (1–4 HD): Fight the bouts the Ring books. Never throw a match it hasn't fixed. Give the Ring its cut of your winnings.

Veteran (5–9 HD): Headline cards and train the up-and-comers. The Ring's bookings come before your own travels.

Champion (10–14 HD): Wear a champion's belt. Your name sells the seats — and the Ring will not let it leave quietly.

Master (15+ HD): Become a name that fills arenas, owned in all but law by the Ring. A champion who walks finds its money and its rivals arrayed against them — to drag them back, or break them.

7. The Greenwarden Lodge

What it is: A fellowship of wardens, scouts, and hunters who keep the wild roads and the frontier safe. (Binds Rangers.)

Alignment: Neutral Good

History: Begun as a handful of trackers guarding a single pass, the Lodge spread along the borderlands — wherever the wild and the settled meet and trouble follows.

Spread: Frontier-wide but thin. There's a waystation at every dangerous crossing, and a network of marked trails only wardens can read.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Recruit (1–4 HD): Walk your patrol and warn the settlements. Never leave a traveller to the wild you could have saved.

Veteran (5–9 HD): Hold a stretch of the frontier and take an apprentice tracker. Answer the Lodge's call to a hunt.

Champion (10–14 HD): Range-master a march and coordinate the wardens. The border's defence comes before your own trail.

Master (15+ HD): Be made a Greenwarden of the inner circle, trusted with the worst threats. The Lodge depends on you. A warden who abandons their march leaves people to die — a thing it neither forgets nor forgives.

8. A Master's Debt

What it is: Not an order but a person — the one who drilled you in your art and made you what you are. (Binds any class.)

Alignment: Any (it matches the master)

History: Yours alone: a hard apprenticeship and a debt of skill that can never quite be repaid.

Spread: A single thread, but a strong one. Your master's name carries weight wherever their students travel.

Demands, by Hit Dice:

Recruit (1–4 HD): Serve when they call. Never raise a hand against them. Pass the art on when asked.

Veteran (5–9 HD): Take their errands and feuds as your own. Honour their name. Return when summoned.

Champion (10–14 HD): Stand as their second and defend their school and students. Their enemies test you first.

Master (15+ HD): You are their heir, expected to carry the art forward and answer for it. To surpass them and then abandon them is the deepest cut a teacher knows. A master scorned can turn every student they ever trained against you.

Appendix H: Multiclass Builds

PACT has no “multiclassing” rule of its own, because every feature already sits on one menu. A “multiclass” character is simply one who buys from more than one class's list.
